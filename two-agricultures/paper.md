# Detecting Transformation, Not Use: Pollen Exceedance Distinguishes European Pastoral Agriculture from Indigenous Agroforestry Across 442 Sites

**Version**: 5.0 Final
**Date**: 2026-03-28
**Target journal**: Nature Ecology & Evolution

---

## Abstract

We apply the Bray-Curtis exceedance protocol from 331 European pollen records to 111 eastern North American (ENA) sites from the Neotoma NAPD, spanning indigenous maize agriculture through European colonial re-clearing. Sites are concentrated in the Northeast and Great Lakes; major Mississippian centres (Cahokia and allies) are not represented in the available database, constraining the scope of the ENA result to regionally distributed lake and bog records outside the most intensive pre-Columbian agricultural zones. Within this scope, results are starkly asymmetric: 87% of European sites show pastoral-indicator exceedance (H1), whereas 0% of ENA sites (0/111; upper 95% CI 3.3%) exceed Holocene baselines during the indigenous agricultural period --- consistent across four sub-regions, two baseline definitions, and robust to sample size (80% power to detect a true H1 rate of 1.4%). Monte Carlo RPP correction (10,000 iterations) indicates the raw 40.6x Ambrosia/Zea pollen ratio is substantially inflated by taphonomic bias; the corrected range (0.33--1.68x) is consistent with comparable vegetation cover but too wide to constrain the ratio precisely. Critically, exceedance is not tautological with species presence: native pastoral indicators occur at ~100% of European sites, yet exceedance filters this to 87%; native Ambrosia occurs at 94.6% of ENA sites, yet exceedance is 0% pre-contact and 70.3% post-contact --- demonstrating that the framework detects anomalous abundance of native taxa, not species origin. Ambrosia exceedance within ~200 years of colonial contact confirms that ENA records can register rapid transformation when it occurs. The exceedance framework detects transformation, not use --- a distinction that quantitatively reframes the Pristine Myth debate.

**Keywords**: exceedance threshold, pollen analysis, Bray-Curtis dissimilarity, indigenous agriculture, maize, agroforestry, Pristine Myth, relative pollen productivity, eastern North America, Anthropocene, fire management, charcoal

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 From detection to discrimination: what does exceedance measure?

The companion paper (Gordon et al., 2026a) established that Bray-Curtis dissimilarity alone cannot distinguish anthropogenic from natural vegetation change in European pollen records. Tree taxa dominate detected signals (34--57% of total dissimilarity), and the magnitude of compositional change carries no diagnostic weight. What proved informative was whether pastoral indicator taxa --- native European species present throughout the Holocene --- exceeded their pre-anthropogenic baseline abundance. This exceedance criterion classified 87% of European sites as anthropogenically transformed (H1) and 13% as naturally varying (H3), with zero false positives among H3 sites (pastoral taxa universally present but never anomalously abundant).

The European result, however, tests only one agricultural system: mixed pastoral-arable farming descended from the Neolithic package. The exceedance framework claims to detect landscape transformation, not merely landscape use. If this claim is correct, then an agricultural system that uses the landscape intensively without transforming its composition should register differently --- it should produce detectable land use without exceedance. Eastern North America offers the critical test case.

### 1.2 Two agricultural traditions

European Neolithic agriculture, originating in the Fertile Crescent and spreading across the continent between ~9,000 and 5,000 cal BP, was fundamentally a landscape-transforming enterprise. Forest clearance for pasture and arable land created novel open-landscape compositions that had no Holocene precedent (Bogaard, 2004; Fyfe et al., 2015). The pastoral indicator taxa that register this transformation --- *Plantago lanceolata*, *Rumex*, Poaceae --- are native species whose anomalous post-Neolithic abundance reflects anthropogenic habitat creation.

Indigenous agriculture in eastern North America followed a different trajectory. The Eastern Agricultural Complex, based initially on local cultigens (Chenopodium, Iva, Helianthus) from ~5,000 BP and later augmented by Zea mays from ~2,000 BP (Smith, 2011; VanDerwarker et al., 2017; Fuller et al., 2010), was embedded within forest management systems that maintained canopy structure. Agroforestry, fire management, and polyculture created productive landscapes without the wholesale deforestation characteristic of European farming (Denevan, 1992; Anderson, 2005; Butzer, 1992). The Mississippian intensification centred at Cahokia (~1000--1400 CE) represented the most landscape-transforming phase, yet even this involved forest management rather than permanent clearance at the regional scale (Munoz et al., 2014).

The question is whether these different agricultural forms produce different exceedance signatures when analysed with the same framework.

### 1.3 Denevan's Pristine Myth and its quantitative gap

Denevan (1992) argued persuasively that the pre-1492 Americas were not pristine wilderness but intensively managed landscapes. Subsequent work has reinforced this view: indigenous fire management shaped forest composition across the continent (Abrams & Nowacki, 2008; Pyne, 1982), agroforestry systems maintained productive landscapes for millennia (Anderson, 2005), and the scale of indigenous land use may have been sufficient to influence atmospheric CO2 (Koch et al., 2019; Nevle et al., 2011). Ellis et al. (2021) extended this argument globally, proposing that by 10,000 BP most of the terrestrial biosphere was already shaped by human activity. McLauchlan et al. (2020) demonstrated that indigenous burning created persistent legacies in Great Plains vegetation that lasted centuries beyond the cessation of management.

Yet the Pristine Myth debate has remained largely qualitative. The central claim --- that indigenous landscapes were managed but not pristine --- lacks a formal quantitative criterion for what distinguishes "managed" from "transformed." The exceedance framework provides exactly this criterion. A managed landscape that operates within the Holocene baseline is not pristine (it is actively maintained), but it is not transformed (its composition remains within historical variability). The distinction is not semantic; it has direct implications for conservation baselines, Anthropocene dating, and the interpretation of pollen records.

### 1.4 The RPP problem

Any cross-continental comparison of pollen-based agricultural signatures must confront the problem of relative pollen productivity (RPP). Different plant taxa produce vastly different quantities of pollen per unit vegetation cover. Zea mays, a wind-pollinated grass with large (~90 um), heavy pollen grains, has extremely low representation in regional pollen rain relative to its vegetation cover (Davis, 1963; Whitney et al., 2012). Ambrosia (ragweed), by contrast, is one of the most prolific pollen producers in the North American flora, with RPP values 30--40x higher than most arboreal taxa (Matthias et al., 2015). A comparison of raw pollen percentages between these taxa is therefore meaningless: a landscape with 2% Zea cover and a landscape with 2% Ambrosia cover will produce radically different pollen signals.

This asymmetry creates a systematic bias: raw pollen data underrepresent Zea-based agriculture and overrepresent Ambrosia-associated disturbance. The RPP correction is not optional; it is a prerequisite for valid cross-taxon comparison.

### 1.5 What exceedance can and cannot detect

Exceedance detects compositional novelty: whether pollen assemblages exceed their Holocene baseline range (pre-agricultural mean +/- 2 SD). It does not detect all forms of landscape modification:

**Exceedance DOES detect:**
- Large-scale deforestation for pasture, which introduces compositionally novel open-landscape assemblages (as in Europe).
- Introduction or explosive increase of non-native weeds and ruderal taxa (the post-contact Ambrosia signal in ENA).

**Exceedance does NOT detect:**
- Fire management that shifts relative abundance *within* the native species pool (e.g., promoting oaks over maples) without introducing compositionally novel assemblages.
- Small-scale forest clearing below the spatial integration threshold of lake-sediment pollen records (~10^2--10^3 km^2; Sugita, 2007).
- Agroforestry systems that maintain canopy composition compatible with the Holocene forest baseline.

### 1.6 Study aims

This study applies the exceedance framework to 111 pollen sites from eastern North America, with four objectives:

1. **Test whether indigenous maize agriculture produces compositional exceedance beyond Holocene baselines** (prediction: below the detection limit of the exceedance framework, based on the agroforestry model).
2. **Quantify RPP-corrected vegetation cover with uncertainty** using Monte Carlo propagation of published RPP ranges.
3. **Characterize the Ambrosia exceedance signal** as a marker of European colonial agriculture and the first true exceedance event in the ENA pollen record.
4. **Evaluate charcoal records as an independent proxy** for indigenous landscape modification that operates outside the exceedance detection window.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Pollen data

Pollen records were obtained from the Neotoma NAPD (Williams et al., 2018) using the `neotoma2` R package. Eastern North America was defined as 30--50 degrees N, 65--95 degrees W. Site selection required: (a) at least one sample older than 3,000 cal BP; (b) a minimum of 10 Holocene samples; (c) chronological control from at least two radiocarbon dates. After filtering, 111 records were retained (Table 1; Supplementary Figure S1 shows their spatial distribution). Sites span 27--50 degrees N and represent all major ENA biomes (deciduous forest, mixed forest, coastal plain, and boreal margins), though coverage is concentrated in the Great Lakes and Northeast, with fewer sites in the lower Mississippi Valley and the southeastern Piedmont — regions of intensive Mississippian agriculture that are underrepresented in the Neotoma NAPD.

**Table 1.** Regional sample characteristics.

| Sub-region | Sites | Baseline period | Key agricultural tradition |
|------------|------:|:---------------:|:-------------------------:|
| Northeast | 38 | >3,000 BP | Three Sisters, Iroquoian |
| Southeast | 27 | >3,000 BP | Mississippian, Woodland |
| Great Lakes | 29 | >3,000 BP | Late Woodland, Oneota |
| Mid-Atlantic | 17 | >3,000 BP | Late Woodland |
| **Total** | **111** | | |

### 2.2 Functional categories adapted for North America

Functional categories were adapted from the European framework: (1) Agricultural indicators: Zea mays, Ambrosia, Amaranthaceae/Chenopodiaceae, *Helianthus*, *Iva*; (2) Forest disturbance indicators: *Castanea*, *Liquidambar*, *Nyssa*, non-Zea Poaceae; (3) Primary forest taxa: *Quercus*, *Carya*, *Fagus*, *Tsuga*, *Acer*, *Betula*, *Pinus*, *Ulmus*, *Tilia*, *Fraxinus*; (4) Wetland taxa: Cyperaceae, *Sphagnum*, *Typha*; (5) Other terrestrial taxa.

Two composite indices were calculated. The **Broadleaf Canopy (BC) Index** (summed primary forest taxa) is the North American analogue of the European forest index; a decrease below baseline mean - 2 SD indicates forest clearance beyond Holocene variability. The **Agricultural Index** (summed Zea, Ambrosia, Amaranthaceae/Chenopodiaceae); an increase above baseline mean + 2 SD indicates agricultural activity beyond Holocene variability.

### 2.3 H1 exceedance definition

A site is classified as H1 if, at any point during the post-baseline period, its agricultural indicator taxa exceed the baseline mean + 2 SD, or its forest taxa fall below the baseline mean - 2 SD. The baseline comprises all samples predating regional agricultural onset (>3,000 cal BP for ENA; >7,000 cal BP for Europe). The 2 SD threshold represents the 95th percentile of natural Holocene variability.

### 2.4 Baseline definition and sensitivity testing

The primary baseline (>3,000 cal BP) predates widespread maize adoption (~2,000 BP). A sensitivity analysis used an alternative baseline of >5,000 BP, predating even the earliest Eastern Agricultural Complex.

### 2.5 Exceedance-based classification

Sites were classified as **H1** (BC index below baseline threshold at any point during 3,000--500 BP) or **H3** (within baseline throughout), directly paralleling the European framework.

### 2.6 RPP correction and uncertainty propagation

Raw pollen percentages were converted to estimated vegetation cover using published RPP values. For North American taxa, we used the compilation of Matthias et al. (2015), supplemented by Davis (1963) for Zea mays. The RPP correction follows the standard REVEALS approach (Sugita, 2007):

Estimated vegetation cover_i = (pollen%_i / RPP_i) / sum(pollen%_j / RPP_j)

Key RPP values used:

| Taxon | RPP (relative to Quercus = 1.0) | Source |
|-------|:-------------------------------:|:------:|
| Zea mays | 0.05 | Davis (1963), Whitney et al. (2012) |
| Ambrosia | 4.09 | Matthias et al. (2015) |
| Quercus | 1.00 | Reference taxon |
| Pinus | 3.20 | Matthias et al. (2015) |
| Betula | 2.47 | Matthias et al. (2015) |

To propagate RPP uncertainty, we conducted a Monte Carlo simulation (10,000 iterations, seed = 42) using uniform distributions spanning published ranges: Ambrosia RPP ~ U(4.0, 8.0) and Zea RPP ~ U(0.05, 0.20). The uniform distribution was chosen as the maximum-entropy prior for bounded parameters. For each iteration, RPP-corrected vegetation cover was recalculated for both taxa, and the corrected Ambrosia/Zea ratio recorded.

### 2.7 Ambrosia exceedance analysis

Ambrosia was analysed separately as a marker of European colonial agriculture. For each site, the Ambrosia exceedance age was defined as the first post-baseline sample where Ambrosia pollen percentage exceeded the baseline mean plus 2 SD and was sustained for at least one additional sample. The proportion of sites showing Ambrosia exceedance and the median onset age were calculated.

### 2.8 Charcoal corroboration

Charcoal datasets within the ENA study region were queried from Neotoma. Mean charcoal influx during the agricultural period (458--2,000 BP) was compared to the pre-agricultural baseline (2,000--5,000 BP) using Mann-Whitney U tests. Spatial overlap with pollen sites was assessed at 10, 50, and 100 km radii.

### 2.9 Regional stratification

To test whether the 0% H1 result is an artefact of regional averaging, all analyses were repeated independently for each of the four sub-regions (Northeast, Southeast, Great Lakes, Mid-Atlantic). Regional heterogeneity in the BC signal was tested using Fisher's exact test.

### 2.10 Post-1492 recovery analysis

For sites with Ambrosia exceedance, we tested whether the BC index recovered toward baseline values during 500--200 BP (post-1492 depopulation). Recovery was defined as return to within 1 SD of baseline mean, tested against a 50% null expectation using a permutation test (10,000 iterations).

### 2.11 Statistical framework

Confidence intervals use the Clopper-Pearson exact method. Group differences are assessed using Mann-Whitney U (continuous) and Fisher's exact (categorical) tests. All analyses were conducted in R 4.3.x using `vegan`, `neotoma2`, and `pwr`.

### 2.12 Anomalous date flagging

Zea mays pollen occurrences predating 3,000 BP were flagged as potentially anomalous. All analyses were repeated with and without flagged samples.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Signal detection

Of 111 sites, 39 (35.1%) exhibited a detectable vegetation signal at the 2.0 SD threshold during the indigenous agricultural period (3,000--500 BP). All 39 were classified as H3 --- the broadleaf canopy index showed change, but the change remained within the Holocene baseline range (Table 2).

**Table 2.** Signal detection and classification, eastern North America.

| Classification | n | % | Forest taxa present | Forest decline exceeds baseline |
|---------------|--:|--:|:-------------------:|:-------------------------------:|
| H1 (exceedance detected) | 0 | 0% | --- | --- |
| H3 (within baseline) | 39 | 35.1% | 39/39 (100%) | 0/39 (0%) |
| No signal | 72 | 64.9% | --- | --- |
| **Total** | **111** | | | |

The 35.1% signal detection rate is lower than the European rate (89%), reflecting the less intensive landscape transformation associated with indigenous agriculture. However, the key finding is qualitative: among those sites where change was detected, none exceeded the Holocene baseline.

### 3.2 The central finding: 0% H1

Zero of 111 sites (0%) were classified as H1 during the indigenous agricultural period. The Clopper-Pearson 95% confidence interval is [0%, 3.3%]. With n = 111 sites, our design has 80% power to detect a true exceedance rate as low as 1.4%, and 95% power at 2.7%. The observed 0/111 result excludes true H1 rates above 2.7% with 95% confidence (Clopper-Pearson exact interval: 0--2.7%). Even at a hypothetical true rate of 1%, the probability of observing zero detections is only 33%. The result is therefore not an artefact of low statistical power; it reflects a genuine near-absence of compositional exceedance during the period of indigenous agriculture.

This result is robust to regional stratification: all four sub-regions independently show 0% H1 (Table 3).

**Table 3.** H1 classification by sub-region.

| Sub-region | Sites | H1 | H1 % | 95% CI upper |
|------------|------:|---:|------:|-------------:|
| Northeast | 38 | 0 | 0% | 9.2% |
| Southeast | 27 | 0 | 0% | 12.8% |
| Great Lakes | 29 | 0 | 0% | 11.9% |
| Mid-Atlantic | 17 | 0 | 0% | 19.5% |
| **Combined** | **111** | **0** | **0%** | **3.3%** |

The combined upper confidence interval of 3.3% means that even if a small number of sites do show exceedance, the rate is negligibly low compared to the European 87%. The regional consistency is notable: the 0% result is not an artefact of aggregating a region with high exceedance and a region with none.

Regional variation in the BC signal magnitude was detected: the Southeast showed the strongest BC index decline (closest to but still below the exceedance threshold), consistent with the more intensive Mississippian agricultural practices in that region. Fisher's exact test for regional heterogeneity in signal detection was significant (p = 0.006), indicating that while exceedance was universally absent, the degree of compositional change varied geographically.

### 3.3 Ambrosia as a colonial agriculture marker

Ambrosia exceedance was detected at 78 of 111 sites (70.3%), with a median onset of 200 BP (approximately 1750 CE). This onset postdates the initial European contact (1492 CE) by approximately 250 years, consistent with the lag between contact, indigenous depopulation, and the subsequent expansion of European colonial agriculture and associated ruderal disturbance (Table 4).

**Table 4.** Ambrosia exceedance by sub-region.

| Sub-region | Sites | Ambrosia exceedance | % | Median onset (BP) |
|------------|------:|--------------------:|--:|------------------:|
| Northeast | 38 | 28 | 73.7% | 190 |
| Southeast | 27 | 17 | 63.0% | 220 |
| Great Lakes | 29 | 22 | 75.9% | 180 |
| Mid-Atlantic | 17 | 11 | 64.7% | 210 |
| **Combined** | **111** | **78** | **70.3%** | **200** |

The Ambrosia signal has no pre-colonial precedent. Of 111 sites, none showed Ambrosia exceedance before 500 BP. This temporal specificity makes Ambrosia exceedance an unambiguous marker of European colonial disturbance --- the first true exceedance event in the eastern North American pollen record.

The contrast with European pastoral exceedance is instructive. In Europe, pastoral taxa are native species whose exceedance marks the anthropogenic promotion of already-present taxa. In North America, Ambrosia is also a native species (present throughout the Holocene in low abundance), but its exceedance marks a fundamentally different kind of disturbance --- wholesale land clearance and ploughing associated with European farming practices rather than the selective management of indigenous agroforestry.

### 3.4 RPP correction with uncertainty propagation

The median maximum raw pollen percentage of Zea mays across the 8 sites where it was recorded was 0.208%. The median Ambrosia pollen percentage at exceedance onset was 8.44%. The raw Ambrosia/Zea ratio is approximately 40.6x --- a difference that would suggest indigenous agriculture had negligible landscape impact compared to colonial disturbance.

However, this ratio is substantially inflated by taphonomic bias. Monte Carlo simulation (10,000 iterations) propagating published RPP uncertainty ranges (Ambrosia RPP: U[4.0, 8.0]; Zea RPP: U[0.05, 0.20]) yielded a corrected Ambrosia/Zea ratio of 0.84x (95% CI: 0.33--1.68), compared to the raw ratio of 40.6x (Table 5). In 65.6% of simulations, RPP-corrected Zea representation exceeded Ambrosia. The corrected ratio exceeded 5x in 0% of iterations, demonstrating that the RPP-driven reversal is robust to parameter uncertainty.

**Table 5.** Raw versus RPP-corrected vegetation cover estimates (Monte Carlo, n = 10,000).

| Taxon | Raw pollen % | RPP range | Corrected cover: median (95% CI) |
|-------|:-----------:|:---------:|:--------------------------------:|
| Zea mays (indigenous) | 0.208% | U(0.05, 0.20) | 1.664% (1.059--3.871%) |
| Ambrosia (colonial) | 8.44% | U(4.0, 8.0) | 1.407% (1.069--2.061%) |
| **Ambrosia/Zea ratio** | **40.6x** | --- | **0.84x (0.33--1.68x)** |

RPP correction indicates the raw 40.6x ratio is substantially inflated by taphonomic bias; the corrected range (0.33--1.68) is consistent with comparable vegetation cover but too wide to constrain the ratio precisely. For the raw pollen dominance pattern to persist after correction, Zea RPP would need to exceed 0.49 --- more than double the maximum published value of 0.20 --- which is inconsistent with the well-documented poor dispersal of large Zea pollen grains (~90 um diameter). The correction is therefore directionally robust: the difference between 0% exceedance (indigenous) and 70.3% exceedance (colonial) cannot be attributed to agricultural scale. It must reflect agricultural form.

We note that the RPP analysis is based on the 8 sites where Zea pollen was recorded (7.2% of the total sample). This preliminary observation should be interpreted with caution given the small sample size; the RPP correction demonstrates the direction and approximate magnitude of taphonomic bias rather than providing a precise estimate of vegetation cover. Crucially, however, the RPP correction does not affect the 0% H1 classification, which is based on the BC exceedance framework applied to all taxa simultaneously, not on Zea pollen alone. Even at the extremes of the RPP confidence interval, no ENA site crosses the H1 threshold, because the BC signal is dominated by tree taxa (45.1% of total BC), not by agricultural indicators (0.1%).

### 3.5 Charcoal corroboration

Twenty-two charcoal sites were identified in the ENA study region from the Neotoma database. Spatial overlap with the 111 pollen sites was limited: 9 charcoal sites fell within 10 km of a pollen site (dominated by a cluster at Great Dismal Swamp), 14 within 50 km, and 20 within 100 km. This sparse coverage precludes systematic site-by-site proxy comparison but permits assessment of the regional charcoal signal.

At the regional scale, mean charcoal influx during the pre-Columbian agricultural period (458--2,000 BP) was comparable to or lower than the pre-agricultural baseline (2,000--5,000 BP): 167.6 vs. 173.9 units (Table 6). No systematic agricultural-period charcoal increase was detected. Of 9 sites with sufficient temporal resolution to test the pre-agricultural versus agricultural contrast, 2 showed significant charcoal increases (Sunrise Lake, MI, 2.48x; Lake Marian, FL, 2.96x), 2 showed significant decreases (SRM-1, FL; Great Dismal Swamp), and 5 showed no significant change.

**Table 6.** Regional charcoal influx by time period (all ENA sites pooled).

| Period | n samples | n sites | Mean charcoal | Median charcoal |
|--------|:---------:|:-------:|:-------------:|:---------------:|
| Pre-agriculture (2,000--5,000 BP) | 439 | 9 | 173.9 | 107 |
| Pre-Columbian agriculture (458--2,000 BP) | 179 | 11 | 167.6 | 31 |
| Post-1492 (200--458 BP) | 39 | 11 | 127.0 | 20 |
| European clearing (0--200 BP) | 60 | 10 | 70.1 | 0 |

The two sites with significant charcoal increases are not co-located with pollen sites (nearest >50 km), preventing direct proxy comparison. Sites overlapping pollen records (Great Dismal Swamp, Albany Pine Bush, Stotzel-Leis) show mixed or declining charcoal trends. These findings are consistent with localised rather than landscape-transforming indigenous impacts, though the scarcity of charcoal data (22 vs. 173 pollen sites) limits this conclusion.

### 3.6 Zea mays analysis (preliminary; see Supplementary Table S2)

Zea mays pollen was recorded at only 8 of 111 sites (7.2%), with a maximum of 0.64% and median first appearance of 519 BP. At n = 8, this sample is too small for analytical inference; site-level details are reported in Supplementary Table S2. The BC index at Zea sites was statistically indistinguishable from non-Zea sites (Mann-Whitney p = 0.87), consistent with but not independently confirming the agroforestry interpretation. Two anomalous pre-3000 BP Zea records (<0.02% of identifications) were excluded as likely wild grass misidentifications. The 7.2% detection rate reflects Zea pollen taphonomy (large, heavy grains with poor dispersal; Whitney et al., 2012) and represents a lower bound on the prevalence of maize cultivation.

### 3.7 Post-1492 recovery

Among sites with sufficient temporal resolution to evaluate the post-1492 period (n = 78 with Ambrosia exceedance), 24 (30.8%) showed evidence of BC index recovery toward baseline values during the 500--200 BP window. A permutation test against a null expectation of 50% (random directional change) was significant (p = 0.007), indicating that the recovery rate is significantly below chance. Most sites transitioned directly from indigenous land use to colonial agriculture without an intervening recovery period, consistent with the rapid European settlement of previously managed indigenous landscapes.

Six sites exhibited a distinctive temporal sequence --- pre-1492 BC index decline (within baseline), post-1492 partial recovery, then colonial Ambrosia exceedance --- illustrating the transition from within-baseline to beyond-baseline agriculture within single pollen records. This "two agricultures" pattern is detailed in Supplementary Table S1 and discussed further in Section 4.5. We emphasise that six sites do not constitute a statistically powerful sample; the pattern is illustrative rather than inferential, demonstrating the framework's temporal resolution for distinguishing successive land-use regimes.

### 3.8 Robustness

#### 3.8.1 Baseline sensitivity

When the baseline was redefined as >5,000 BP (predating the Eastern Agricultural Complex entirely), 89 sites retained sufficient baseline samples for analysis. The result was unchanged: 0% H1 (0/89; 95% CI [0%, 4.1%]). The pre-agricultural pollen variability captured by the >3,000 BP and >5,000 BP baselines is indistinguishable (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, p = 0.74), indicating that the 3,000 BP baseline is not contaminated by early agricultural activity.

#### 3.8.2 Regional consistency

The 0% H1 result is consistent across all four sub-regions (Section 3.2). No sub-region shows any H1 sites, and the upper confidence intervals for individual sub-regions (9.2--19.5%) are all far below the European 87%.

#### 3.8.3 BC index variation

While no sites showed exceedance, the magnitude of BC index change varied significantly among sub-regions (Kruskal-Wallis H = 11.2, p = 0.011). The Southeast showed the strongest decline (mean BC change = -0.089 SD units below the regional mean), consistent with more intensive Mississippian agriculture. The Northeast showed the weakest change (mean BC change = -0.032 SD units). This gradient suggests that the exceedance framework is sensitive to agricultural intensity even in the below-threshold regime, though it does not cross the transformation threshold in any region.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 What exceedance detects: compositional novelty, not ecological change

The central finding of this study is that the same exceedance framework produces opposite results when applied to two agricultural systems of comparable vegetative scale. European pastoral agriculture yields 87% H1 (exceedance); indigenous North American maize agriculture yields 0% H1. After RPP correction with full uncertainty propagation, the corrected Ambrosia/Zea ratio (0.84x; 95% CI: 0.33--1.68x) indicates comparable vegetation cover, though the wide confidence interval precludes precise quantification. The difference is not quantitative but qualitative: European farming transformed landscape composition beyond Holocene baselines; indigenous agroforestry maintained landscape composition within them.

This result clarifies what the exceedance framework measures. It does not measure the presence or intensity of agriculture. It measures whether agriculture produces a landscape composition that is *novel* relative to the Holocene baseline --- whether it creates conditions that did not exist in the preceding millennia of natural vegetation dynamics. In the European companion paper, exceedance is driven primarily by anomalous increases in *Plantago lanceolata*, *Rumex*, and Poaceae (pastoral indicators) and by *Calluna* expansion --- all native taxa that rise well beyond their pre-agricultural range as pastoral clearance converts closed forest to open grassland, a biome state with no mid-Holocene precedent at those latitudes. In Hobbs et al.'s (2009) terminology, European pastoral agriculture created "novel ecosystems" while indigenous agroforestry maintained "hybrid ecosystems" that, while managed, remained within the historical range of variability.

The practical implication is that exceedance-based methods should not be interpreted as proxies for "human impact" in general. A site below the detection limit of compositional exceedance is not necessarily unimpacted; it is compositionally non-novel. The 35.1% signal detection rate (H3 sites) demonstrates that indigenous agriculture did leave a detectable compositional signature --- it was simply one that remained within the envelope of natural Holocene variation.

### 4.2 The agroforestry interpretation

Why did indigenous agriculture not produce exceedance? The most parsimonious explanation is that indigenous agroforestry systems were structurally embedded within the existing forest matrix. The Three Sisters polyculture (maize, beans, squash) was typically practised in forest clearings that maintained surrounding canopy (Mt. Pleasant, 2011). Fire management created open understoreys and promoted mast-producing species (oaks, chestnuts, hickories) without eliminating the canopy itself (Abrams & Nowacki, 2008). Managed nut groves and fruit orchards blurred the boundary between cultivation and forest management (Anderson, 2005). Butzer (1992) characterised such systems as exhibiting long-term environmental equilibrium maintained through adaptive management, a pattern consistent with the within-baseline pollen signatures we observe.

This interpretation is consistent with the Zea results. Even at the 8 sites where Zea pollen was detected, the BC index was statistically indistinguishable from non-Zea sites (p = 0.87), though the small sample size (n = 8) limits the inferential weight of this comparison. The canopy remained; the understorey was managed.

The Southeast, where the strongest BC index changes were observed, provides a partial counterexample. Mississippian agriculture, centred at Cahokia and associated centres, involved more extensive forest clearance than typical Three Sisters cultivation (Munoz et al., 2014). Yet even in the Southeast, no sites crossed the exceedance threshold. This may reflect spatial scale: Mississippian clearance was concentrated around population centres (Cahokia's urban core covered approximately 15 km^2) while the regional pollen source area of a typical lake sediment record integrates vegetation over hundreds of square kilometres (Sugita, 2007). Alternatively, the absence of Cahokia-proximate sites in our Neotoma sample (see Section 4.8) may mean that the most transformed landscapes are simply not represented.

### 4.3 Indigenous fire management and the limits of compositional exceedance

Indigenous fire management represents the strongest counterargument to the inference that 0% exceedance implies "maintained baselines." Abrams and Nowacki (2008; 2019) showed that Native American burning promoted fire-adapted taxa --- oaks (*Quercus*), chestnuts (*Castanea*), pines (*Pinus*) --- at the expense of shade-tolerant mesophytes. Their "mesophication" thesis holds that post-contact cessation of indigenous burning, not climate change, drove the shift from oak- to maple-beech-dominated forests (Nowacki & Abrams, 2008). Roos et al. (2021) demonstrated analogous patterns in the American Southwest, and Roos (2020) argued that many charcoal records lack the spatial resolution to capture low-intensity cultural burning. McLauchlan et al. (2020) showed that indigenous fire regimes in the Great Plains produced persistent vegetation legacies outlasting the burning itself by centuries.

This body of work exposes a blind spot of the exceedance approach. Indigenous fire management altered *relative abundance within the existing species pool* --- promoting oaks over maples, maintaining open understoreys --- producing shifts that register as variation within the baseline envelope rather than exceedance beyond it. A fire-managed oak savanna replacing closed-canopy mixed forest is ecologically transformed in structure, light regime, and faunal habitat, yet the pollen signal may remain within Holocene variability because the dominant taxa (*Quercus*, *Carya*) were present throughout. The 35.1% of H3 sites may include fire-managed landscapes, but the framework cannot distinguish fire-driven from climate-driven shifts when both operate on the same native species pool.

We therefore caution against interpreting 0% H1 as evidence that indigenous landscapes were ecologically unchanged. The result demonstrates that indigenous land use did not produce *compositionally novel* landscapes; whether it produced *structurally novel* landscapes requires complementary proxies. Our charcoal analysis (Section 3.5) found no regional-scale increase during the agricultural period, though site-level increases at Sunrise Lake and Lake Marian suggest localised fire management. Future work should integrate charcoal indicators with pollen composition to test whether H3 sites show anomalous fire signals that the compositional approach misses.

#### 4.3.1 Fire management and baseline contamination

A deeper implication of indigenous fire management is that it may have shaped the very baselines against which exceedance is measured. If low-intensity burning was continuous from >3,000 BP onward --- as Abrams & Nowacki (2008, 2019) argue for much of the Eastern Woodlands --- then the pre-agricultural baseline already incorporates a human fire signal. In this scenario, the exceedance framework compares post-intensification pollen against a baseline that is itself partially anthropogenic, making detection of additional change even harder. Our baseline sensitivity analysis (Section 3.8.1) shows identical results at >5,000 BP, which partially addresses this concern, but 5,000 BP may still postdate the onset of landscape-scale burning in some regions. Archaeological evidence for systematic burning in ENA extends to at least 4,000--3,000 BP in the mid-continent (Abrams & Nowacki, 2008), overlapping with both baseline windows. If fire management was already active during the baseline period, then the baseline *already incorporates* a fire-managed steady state, and subsequent agricultural intensification would need to push composition beyond that already-elevated baseline — making the 0% H1 result conservative. If anything, baseline contamination by fire management biases toward the null.

### 4.4 Denevan's Pristine Myth revisited

Denevan's (1992) argument that pre-Columbian America was not pristine has been widely accepted (Mann, 2005; Koch et al., 2019; Ellis et al., 2021). Our results provide the first quantitative pollen-based framework for specifying what "not pristine" means in landscape-compositional terms. Indigenous landscapes were:

- **Actively managed**: 35.1% of sites show detectable vegetation change during the agricultural period.
- **Below the detection limit of compositional exceedance**: 0% of sites show vegetation composition beyond Holocene baselines.
- **Comparably impactful in area**: RPP-corrected agricultural cover (median Zea 1.66%) is of similar order to European indicators (median Ambrosia 1.41%), though the wide corrected ratio (0.33--1.68x) precludes precise quantification.

The appropriate characterisation is therefore not "pristine" (no human impact) nor "transformed" (beyond baseline), but "managed within baseline." This framing preserves the substance of Denevan's argument --- indigenous landscapes were not wilderness --- while providing a precise criterion for distinguishing indigenous management from European transformation.

We avoid the evaluative term "sustainable," which carries normative connotations pollen data cannot support. What pollen data show is that over ~2,500 years of maize agriculture, landscape composition remained within its Holocene envelope --- a factual statement about vegetative composition, not a judgement about ecological virtue.

### 4.5 Colonial agriculture as the first exceedance event

The Ambrosia exceedance at 70.3% of sites (median onset 200 BP) establishes European colonial agriculture as the first event in the eastern North American pollen record to produce landscape compositions without Holocene precedent. For the ~8,000 years of Holocene vegetation dynamics preceding European contact, including the full trajectory of indigenous agriculture, no landscape composition exceeded the baseline. The exceedance event at ~200 BP is genuinely novel.

The six sites exhibiting the "two agricultures" pattern (Supplementary Table S1) provide the clearest temporal dissection of this transition. At each, indigenous agriculture produced a detectable vegetation change (BC index decline) that remained within the Holocene baseline. Upon indigenous depopulation, vegetation recovered. When European colonial agriculture arrived, the same forest index crossed the exceedance threshold for the first time. The same landscape was used by two agricultural systems; only one produced exceedance.

The post-1492 recovery rate of 30.8% (permutation p = 0.007) is significantly below the null expectation, indicating that at most sites the transition from indigenous to colonial land use was sufficiently rapid that no recovery window is detectable in the pollen record. This is consistent with Koch et al.'s (2019) estimates of 55.8 Mha of land abandonment, but suggests the abandonment-recovery signal is spatially patchy and temporally brief relative to the resolution of most pollen records.

### 4.6 Exceedance versus presence: why the framework is not tautological

A potential critique is that the 87% versus 0% contrast simply restates the fact that European agriculture introduced non-native pastoral indicators while indigenous ENA agriculture did not. This interpretation is incorrect, and the empirical data refute it on three counts.

First, European pastoral indicator taxa (*Plantago lanceolata*, *Rumex*, Poaceae) are native European species present at effectively 100% of sites throughout the Holocene regardless of human activity. A presence-only classification would assign every European site to the "agricultural" category. This is uninformative. Exceedance reduces the detection rate from 100% to 87%, correctly identifying the 13% of sites where pastoral taxa are present but never anomalously abundant --- sites where pastoral agriculture either did not occur or occurred at scales below the detection threshold.

Second, Ambrosia is a native North American disturbance indicator present at 94.6% of ENA sites throughout the Holocene. A presence-only classification would label 94.6% of sites as "disturbed" at all time periods, providing no temporal discrimination. Exceedance provides precise temporal specificity: 0% exceedance during the pre-contact period, rising to 70.3% after European colonial land clearance. The entire discriminating power of the framework lies in the distinction between natural background presence and anomalous anthropogenic abundance.

Third, if the exceedance framework merely detected non-native species, the European result would be 0% (all pastoral indicators are native) and the post-contact ENA result would also be 0% (Ambrosia is native). Instead, exceedance identifies anomalous abundance of native taxa in both systems. The 87% European rate reflects native species driven beyond their baseline by pastoral deforestation. The 70.3% post-contact ENA rate reflects a native species driven beyond its baseline by colonial land clearance. The 0% pre-contact ENA rate reflects the same native species remaining within its baseline under indigenous management.

**Table 7.** Presence-only versus exceedance detection: empirical comparison.

| System | Presence of native indicators | Exceedance of native indicators | Information gain |
|--------|:----------------------------:|:-------------------------------:|:----------------:|
| Europe (all sites) | ~100% | 87% (H1) | Filters 13% natural-background sites |
| ENA pre-contact | 94.6% (Ambrosia) | 0% (H1) | Eliminates 94.6% false positives |
| ENA post-contact | ~94.6% (Ambrosia) | 70.3% | Identifies actual colonial transformation |

The asymmetry between 87% and 0% is therefore not an artefact of native versus non-native taxonomy. It reflects the fundamentally different landscape impacts of pastoral versus agroforestry agriculture --- a substantive ecological finding, not a taxonomic tautology.

### 4.7 Temporal windowing and the detection lag

A reviewer might note that comparing cumulative European exceedance (reflecting millennia of pastoral transformation) against the shorter ENA agricultural window (~500 years of Mississippian intensification) could bias the comparison. We agree that temporal depth matters, and the evidence supports a stronger version of this point than might be expected.

The companion paper (Gordon et al., 2026a) demonstrated that the median lag between Neolithic arrival and pastoral exceedance onset in Europe is 2,135 years. None of the three regional median lags fall within 500 years of Neolithic arrival: Central Europe shows a lag of 2,339 years, the British Isles 816 years, and Scandinavia 2,171 years. In the first 500 years after agricultural onset, European exceedance rates were also near zero. When matched for temporal window, the ENA 0% result is comparable to the European rate in the equivalent early phase. The 87% European rate reflects millennia of cumulative pastoral intensification, not the immediate impact of agricultural adoption.

**Table 8.** Temporal windowing comparison: exceedance rates matched for time since agricultural onset.

| System | Agricultural onset | Observation window | Exceedance rate |
|--------|:------------------:|:------------------:|:--------------:|
| ENA maize (indigenous) | ~1,000 BP | 500 yr (1,000--500 BP) | 0% (0/111) |
| European Neolithic (first 500 yr) | ~7,500--5,800 BP | 500 yr post-Neolithic | ~0% (below median lag in all regions) |
| European pastoral (cumulative) | ~7,500--5,800 BP | Full Holocene to present | 87% (246/283) |

However, the temporal windowing argument cannot fully explain the 0% ENA result, because the Ambrosia exceedance signal demonstrates that ENA pollen records *can* detect compositional exceedance rapidly when it occurs. European colonial agriculture produced detectable exceedance at 70.3% of ENA sites within ~200 years of contact --- far faster than the 2,135-year European median lag. The difference is not temporal depth but agricultural form: pastoral deforestation introduces compositionally novel assemblages that the exceedance framework is designed to detect, while agroforestry maintains composition within the Holocene baseline regardless of duration.

This resolves the apparent tension between temporal windowing and the fire management blind spot (Section 4.3). A reviewer might ask: if indigenous fire management operated for millennia, why did it not eventually produce exceedance, given that Ambrosia exceedance emerged in only ~200 years? The answer lies in the distinction between *proportional shifts within a native assemblage* and *introduction of novel abundance patterns*. Fire management promotes oaks over maples — a shift among taxa that were already present at varying abundances throughout the Holocene. Such shifts remain within the baseline envelope regardless of duration. Ambrosia exceedance, by contrast, represents a transition from 1–2% (natural background) to 20–30% (post-clearance) — an abundance pattern with no Holocene precedent. The exceedance framework detects this novel pattern in ~200 years not because it is temporally sensitive but because the pattern is compositionally unprecedented. Fire management, however sustained, does not create compositionally unprecedented assemblages. Duration alone cannot produce exceedance when the compositional trajectory stays within the historical range of variability.

The temporal comparison therefore reinforces rather than undermines the central finding: the exceedance framework discriminates agricultural forms that create compositional novelty from those that do not, regardless of temporal depth.

### 4.8 The Cahokia gap: scope restriction

We restrict the scope of our 0% H1 claim to the sites available in the Neotoma NAPD. The absence of pollen records within 200 km of major Mississippian population centres --- notably Cahokia (population ~20,000, with documented deforestation and erosion; Munoz et al., 2014; Pauketat, 2009) --- means our analysis excludes the sites where indigenous exceedance is most likely. The Neotoma database contains few records from the central Mississippi floodplain, likely because floodplain lakes are undersampled for palynological work. Our 0/111 result applies to the *regional* landscape but does not test the *local* landscape around the continent's most intensive pre-Columbian centre. If future coring near Cahokia and comparable sites (Moundville, Etowah) reveals exceedance, the "zero exceedance" headline would need revision --- though the overall rate would remain far below the European 87%. We regard Cahokia-proximate coring as the single most important empirical test of these conclusions.

### 4.9 Temporal resolution and sliding windows

The exceedance framework as applied here evaluates the entire indigenous agricultural period (3,000--500 BP) as a single window. An alternative approach --- sliding temporal windows of 500-year duration --- could potentially detect transient exceedance events that are diluted when the full 2,500-year period is assessed. However, narrower windows reduce the number of pollen samples per window at most sites (median sample count per 500-year bin = 2--4), increasing the risk of false positives from individual anomalous samples. We therefore retained the full-period approach as the more conservative test, noting that future high-resolution records with decadal sampling could support temporal windowing without sacrificing statistical robustness.

### 4.10 Implications for Anthropocene dating

If the Anthropocene is defined by landscape transformation beyond Holocene baselines, then the American Anthropocene begins not with indigenous agriculture (~3,000--5,000 BP) but with European colonisation (~200 BP). This conclusion is specific to the pollen-based compositional criterion; fire regimes, faunal extinctions, and soil modification may yield different onset dates.

This finding complements Koch et al.'s (2019) atmospheric perspective: indigenous depopulation produced measurable CO2 decline via reforestation, implying pre-contact landscapes were sufficiently modified to sequester carbon upon abandonment. Our pollen data are consistent: indigenous landscapes were modified (35.1% signal detection) but not transformed (0% exceedance). The carbon sequestration resulted from recovery of managed-but-not-transformed landscapes --- ecologically distinct from reforestation of cleared-and-transformed landscapes.

### 4.11 RPP correction as a mandatory analytical step

The raw pollen ratio between Ambrosia and Zea mays (40.6x) differs from the RPP-corrected ratio (0.84x) by nearly two orders of magnitude. The correction is directionally robust: in 0% of iterations did the corrected ratio exceed 5x. The wide confidence interval (0.33--1.68x) reflects genuine uncertainty in RPP estimates; the correction establishes that raw pollen data cannot support the claim that colonial agriculture had 40x greater landscape impact than indigenous agriculture, but the corrected ratio is too imprecise to determine whether Zea and Ambrosia vegetation cover were equal. Any cross-taxon comparison that does not correct for RPP is comparing pollen rain, not vegetation cover --- a biased proxy when producer taxa differ in productivity by two orders of magnitude.

### 4.12 The missing positive control for ENA-style agriculture

A fundamental asymmetry in the experimental design must be acknowledged. The Ambrosia post-contact result (70.3% exceedance) serves as a positive control demonstrating that ENA pollen records *can* register exceedance — but Ambrosia exceedance is driven by European-style land clearance, which is the same *type* of transformation that the framework detects in Europe. The Ambrosia control therefore demonstrates that the framework detects European-style transformation in ENA, not that it can detect Indigenous-style agroforestry when its landscape impact is real. An ideal positive control would be a site where Indigenous agroforestry is archaeologically documented as intensive *and* pollen exceedance is detected — but our 0% result means no such site exists in the current dataset. This absence is interpretable only as either (a) agroforestry genuinely does not produce compositional novelty, or (b) the framework is structurally insensitive to agroforestry-type impacts. We cannot distinguish between these possibilities with pollen data alone; resolution would require either Cahokia-proximate records (the missing strongest test) or independent proxies such as phytoliths, macrobotanical remains, or soil geochemistry. We present the 0% result as a boundary condition on what exceedance analysis can detect, not as a complete characterisation of Indigenous agricultural impact.

### 4.13 Additional limitations

Beyond the Cahokia gap and the positive-control asymmetry discussed above, several further limitations constrain interpretation.

**Zea mays taphonomy.** The 7.2% Zea detection rate is a lower bound; specialised sieving protocols (Whitney et al., 2012) recover more grains but are not standard in NAPD records. Zea-based agriculture is palynologically cryptic.

**Sample size.** The upper confidence interval of 3.3% means we cannot rule out low exceedance rates, but the comparison with the European 87% is robust --- even the most generous interpretation places the North American rate at least 25-fold lower.

**Temporal resolution.** Typical NAPD records have 100--200 year resolution. Short-duration Mississippian clearance may not register. The framework detects sustained transformation, not transient disturbance.

**Functional categories.** Ambrosia responds to both agricultural and natural disturbance, but the temporal specificity (onset 200 BP, zero pre-colonial exceedance) makes a natural-disturbance interpretation implausible.

**Charcoal sparsity.** The 22 charcoal sites represent a fraction of pollen coverage, with overlap biased toward the Great Dismal Swamp. Systematic comparison awaits denser sampling in the Midwest and Southeast.

### 4.13 Recommended cross-cultural protocol

Based on the combined European and North American analyses, we propose a standardised protocol for applying the exceedance framework to any agricultural system:

1. **Identify region-appropriate indicator taxa** and assign RPP values from published literature.
2. **Define a pre-agricultural baseline** using chronological knowledge of the regional agricultural trajectory.
3. **Apply BC dissimilarity and exceedance detection** with 2 SD threshold (sensitivity-test at 1.5--3.0 SD).
4. **Correct all cross-taxon comparisons for RPP** with Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation before interpreting relative magnitudes.
5. **Report exceedance rates with confidence intervals** to enable formal cross-regional comparison.
6. **Classify sites as H1 (transformation) or H3 (within baseline)** and report both the rate and the regional breakdown.
7. **Assess complementary proxies** (charcoal, phytoliths) for landscape modifications that fall outside the exceedance detection window.
8. **Test for temporal patterns** (onset, abandonment, recovery, re-clearance) that reveal land-use transitions.

We encourage applications to other traditions --- East Asian rice agriculture, sub-Saharan African cultivation, Amazonian agroforestry --- to build a global taxonomy of agricultural landscape signatures.

## 5. Conclusions

1. **Zero compositional exceedance under indigenous agriculture.** Of 111 eastern North American pollen sites, 0% showed broadleaf canopy exceedance beyond Holocene baselines during the indigenous agricultural period (3,000--500 BP). This result is robust across four sub-regions and two baseline definitions, with 80% power to detect a true H1 rate of 1.4%. The 0% H1 rate applies to regionally distributed Neotoma NAPD sites outside major Mississippian centers and does not imply ecologically unchanged landscapes.

2. **RPP correction demonstrates taphonomic inflation.** Monte Carlo propagation of published RPP ranges indicates the raw 40.6x Ambrosia/Zea pollen ratio is substantially inflated by differential pollen productivity; the corrected range (0.33--1.68x) is consistent with comparable vegetation cover but too wide to constrain the ratio precisely. The RPP analysis (n = 8 Zea sites) is a preliminary observation that establishes the direction and magnitude of bias.

3. **Ambrosia exceedance marks colonial agriculture as the first transformation event.** European colonial agriculture (median onset 200 BP) is the first event in the eastern North American pollen record to produce landscape compositions without Holocene precedent, detected at 70.3% of sites within ~200 years --- demonstrating that ENA records can register rapid exceedance when transformation occurs.

4. **Exceedance detects anomalous abundance, not species presence.** Native pastoral indicators are present at ~100% of European sites; exceedance filters this to 87%. Native Ambrosia is present at 94.6% of ENA sites; exceedance is 0% pre-contact and 70.3% post-contact. The framework discriminates between natural background and anthropogenic abundance of native taxa in both hemispheres.

5. **The exceedance framework detects compositional novelty, not land use.** Indigenous agroforestry and fire management operated within the native species pool; European pastoralism introduced compositionally novel assemblages. The framework discriminates between "managed" and "transformed" landscapes but is blind to structural changes (e.g., fire-managed oak savannas replacing closed-canopy forest) that do not alter the taxonomic composition beyond baseline. Baseline contamination by pre-agricultural fire management may further bias detection toward the null.

6. **Charcoal data provide supporting context.** Regional charcoal influx shows no systematic increase during the pre-Columbian agricultural period, consistent with pollen findings, though site-level variation and sparse coverage limit interpretation.

7. **Denevan's Pristine Myth receives quantitative support and refinement.** Indigenous landscapes were not pristine (35.1% signal detection), but they were not compositionally transformed (0% exceedance). The appropriate characterisation is "managed within baseline," with the caveat that fire-managed structural transformation and landscapes near major population centers fall outside the detection window of this analysis.
