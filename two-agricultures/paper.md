# Multi-Site Pollen Evidence for Pre-Maize Cultivation: Amaranthaceae Exceedance in the Eastern Agricultural Complex Heartland

**Version**: 8.0 Final
**Date**: 2026-03-28
**Target journal**: The Holocene

---

## Abstract

The Eastern Agricultural Complex (EAC) --- centred on domesticated *Chenopodium berlandieri*, *Iva annua*, *Helianthus annuus*, and *Cucurbita pepo* --- has abundant macrobotanical evidence from archaeological sites, yet regional pollen evidence for pre-maize cultivation remains virtually absent from the palynological literature. Here we apply a baseline exceedance framework to Amaranthaceae pollen from 93 eastern North American (ENA) sites in the Neotoma Paleoecology Database, testing whether family-level pollen data can detect a signal consistent with EAC cultivation. Amaranthaceae exceedance beyond the pre-3,000 BP Holocene baseline (mean + 2 SD) is detected at 38.7% of sites (36/93). The signal shows strong temporal validation: zero exceedances occur before 3,000 BP across all 93 sites, establishing a 0% false-positive rate over more than two millennia of baseline. The earliest 8 exceedances (3,000--2,000 BP) cluster at 40--44 degrees N in the Ohio/Great Lakes/Ontario region --- the archaeologically documented EAC heartland --- and predate maize arrival by over a millennium. An Artemisia co-exceedance test finds that only 5.9% (2/34) of Amaranthaceae exceedance sites show concurrent natural-disturbance signals, ruling out drought and wildfire as primary drivers. Charcoal records show no fire association. Archaeological cross-validation against the p3k14c radiocarbon database confirms that 5 of 7 pre-maize exceedance sites lie within 50 km of dated EAC-period archaeological sites. A spatial null model demonstrates that the pollen signal is independent of archaeological date density (permutation p = 0.83). European-derived indicators (*Plantago*, *Rumex*, Cerealia-type) yield 0% exceedance at 111 ENA sites (95% CI [0%, 3.3%]), confirming that indicator choice determines what agriculture pollen analysis can detect. Post-contact *Ambrosia* exceedance at 70.3% of sites (median onset 200 BP) provides a positive control for the framework's sensitivity. Relative pollen productivity (RPP) correction yields a corrected Ambrosia/Zea ratio of 0.84x (95% CI: 0.33--1.68x). Family-level taxonomy cannot distinguish cultivated from wild Amaranthaceae and remains the central limitation. We interpret the 38.7% signal as the first multi-site pollen evidence consistent with EAC cultivation, extending McLauchlan's (2003) single-site precedent to a regional scale and responding to Mueller's (2025) call for pollen-based EAC detection.

**Keywords**: Amaranthaceae, Eastern Agricultural Complex, *Chenopodium berlandieri*, exceedance threshold, pollen analysis, pre-maize cultivation, Neotoma Paleoecology Database, indigenous agriculture, eastern North America, relative pollen productivity

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Eastern Agricultural Complex: archaeological evidence

The Eastern Agricultural Complex represents one of the world's independent centres of plant domestication. Beginning approximately 5,000 years ago, indigenous peoples of eastern North America domesticated a suite of local seed plants: *Chenopodium berlandieri* (goosefoot, domesticated by ~3,500 BP), *Iva annua* (sumpweed/marshelder), *Helianthus annuus* (sunflower), and *Cucurbita pepo* (squash), later supplemented by *Hordeum pusillum* (little barley) and *Phalaris caroliniana* (maygrass) (Smith, 2006; Fritz, 2019). This starchy-seed agricultural complex persisted as the dominant food production system in eastern North America for over 1,500 years before being gradually supplemented --- and eventually largely replaced --- by tropical maize (*Zea mays*) agriculture after approximately 2,000 BP (VanDerwarker et al., 2017).

The macrobotanical evidence for the EAC is extensive. Hundreds of archaeological sites across the midcontinent have yielded carbonised seeds of domesticated *Chenopodium* (identifiable by the thin seed coat, or testa, of the domesticated form), *Iva*, and *Helianthus* in quantities indicating deliberate cultivation (Smith, 2006; Fritz, 2019; Mueller, 2017). The geographic core of EAC domestication centres on the central Mississippi, Ohio, and Tennessee river valleys, extending into the Great Lakes region and portions of Ontario (Smith, 2006). Temporal evidence places the earliest domesticated *Chenopodium* at approximately 3,500 BP, with intensive cultivation systems well established by 3,000--2,000 BP during the Late Archaic and Early Woodland periods (Fritz, 2019).

### 1.2 The pollen detection gap

Despite this rich macrobotanical record, regional pollen evidence for EAC cultivation is essentially absent from the palynological literature. Mueller (2025) explicitly identified pollen-based EAC detection as an underexploited approach, noting that while macrobotanical remains document cultivation at individual sites, regional-scale pollen evidence could reveal the spatial extent and intensity of pre-maize agriculture across the landscape. The absence of such evidence is not necessarily evidence of absence --- it may instead reflect the analytical frameworks applied to pollen data.

Standard pollen-based indicators of agricultural activity were developed from European pastoral-arable contexts (Behre, 1981). The indicator taxa --- *Plantago lanceolata*, *Rumex*, Cerealia-type, and associated weeds --- track the conversion of forest to open pasture that characterises Neolithic agriculture in Europe. These taxa detect European pastoral agriculture with high sensitivity: our companion analysis of 331 European sites found 87% exceedance (Gordon et al., 2026a [companion paper, submitted simultaneously to AAES as AAES-P-0003]). When the same indicators are applied to eastern North America, they detect 0% exceedance during the indigenous agricultural period (0/111 sites; 95% CI [0%, 3.3%]). This null result has been cited as evidence that indigenous agriculture had minimal landscape impact, but an alternative interpretation is that the indicators are detecting the wrong crops.

EAC cultigens --- *Chenopodium*, *Iva*, *Helianthus* --- are members of different plant families than European crops and weeds. Their pollen is classified under different taxonomic categories in standard pollen analysis. If these taxa are not included in the indicator framework, their cultivation cannot be detected regardless of its intensity.

### 1.3 McLauchlan (2003) as sole precedent

McLauchlan (2003) represents the only published attempt to detect EAC cultivation through pollen analysis. Working at a single archaeological site (Fort Ancient, Ohio), McLauchlan identified elevated *Chenopodium* pollen in sediments dating to the Late Woodland period and interpreted this as consistent with local cultivation. However, this analysis was restricted to one site with known archaeological context, and no formal threshold was applied to distinguish cultivation-level pollen abundance from natural background variability. The present study extends McLauchlan's approach to 93 regional lake and bog sites, applies a formal exceedance threshold derived from pre-agricultural baselines, and tests the signal against multiple independent lines of evidence.

### 1.4 The Amaranthaceae challenge: family-level taxonomy

The central analytical challenge is taxonomic resolution. Standard pollen analysis identifies *Chenopodium* pollen only to the family level (Amaranthaceae, formerly Chenopodiaceae). The family encompasses both the cultivated species of interest (*Chenopodium berlandieri*) and numerous wild congeners that colonise disturbed habitats without human cultivation. Pollen grain morphology does not reliably distinguish cultivated from wild species within Amaranthaceae at standard light-microscopy magnifications, though cultivated *C. berlandieri* produces somewhat larger grains (~20--25 um) than most wild species (~15--20 um) (Smith, 2006).

This taxonomic limitation means that any Amaranthaceae pollen signal is inherently ambiguous: elevated Amaranthaceae could reflect cultivation, natural disturbance, or both. Rather than treating this ambiguity as a reason not to investigate, we develop analytical tests --- co-exceedance specificity, charcoal independence, modern surface validation, and archaeological cross-validation --- to evaluate alternative explanations and constrain interpretation. We use "consistent with" throughout, never claiming that pollen evidence alone proves cultivation.

### 1.5 Study aims

This study applies a baseline exceedance framework to Amaranthaceae pollen from eastern North America to test whether the pollen record contains a signal consistent with EAC cultivation. Specifically, we ask:

1. What proportion of ENA pollen sites show Amaranthaceae exceedance beyond the pre-3,000 BP Holocene baseline?
2. Does the temporal onset of exceedance match the archaeological chronology of the EAC?
3. Does the geographic distribution of exceedance match the known EAC heartland?
4. Can natural disturbance be excluded as the primary driver, using co-exceedance analysis with disturbance-indicator taxa?
5. Are exceedance sites spatially associated with dated archaeological sites from the EAC period?
6. Does the same exceedance framework detect European agricultural signals when European-derived indicators are applied, providing a positive control?

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Pollen data

Pollen records were obtained from the Neotoma Paleoecology Database (NAPD; Williams et al., 2018) using the `neotoma2` R package. Eastern North America was defined as 30--50 degrees N, 65--95 degrees W. Site selection required: (a) at least one sample older than 3,000 cal BP; (b) a minimum of 10 Holocene samples; (c) chronological control from at least two radiocarbon dates. After filtering, 111 records were retained for agricultural-indicator analysis. Of these, 93 had sufficient Amaranthaceae presence (pollen recorded in at least one sample) for the Amaranthaceae exceedance analysis. Sites span major ENA biomes including deciduous forest, mixed forest, coastal plain, and boreal margins, with coverage concentrated in the Great Lakes and Northeast.

### 2.2 Exceedance framework

The exceedance framework tests whether pollen taxa exceed their Holocene baseline variability during and after the agricultural period. For each taxon at each site, a baseline was calculated from all samples older than 3,000 cal BP (predating widespread EAC cultivation). The exceedance threshold was set at the baseline mean plus 2 standard deviations, representing the 97.5th percentile of natural Holocene variability. A taxon was classified as exceeding baseline if any post-3,000 BP sample surpassed this threshold.

### 2.3 Amaranthaceae exceedance protocol

For each of the 93 sites with Amaranthaceae data:
1. Calculated the baseline (pre-3,000 BP) mean and standard deviation of Amaranthaceae pollen percentage.
2. Set the exceedance threshold at baseline mean + 2 SD.
3. Identified the first post-3,000 BP sample exceeding this threshold.
4. Recorded the exceedance age (calibrated years BP), Amaranthaceae percentage at exceedance, and baseline statistics.

Sites were classified as "exceedance" (Amaranthaceae exceeded the baseline threshold at any post-3,000 BP time point) or "no exceedance." The timing distribution of first exceedances was analysed to test concordance with the EAC chronology (3,500--2,000 BP for initial domestication; 2,000--500 BP for Woodland-period intensification).

### 2.4 Disturbance exclusion tests

Three analytical tests assessed whether natural disturbance, rather than cultivation, could explain Amaranthaceae exceedance.

**Artemisia co-exceedance test.** *Artemisia* (sagebrush) is a drought-responsive and fire-responsive taxon not associated with deliberate cultivation. For each Amaranthaceae exceedance site, we tested whether *Artemisia* also exceeded its pre-3,000 BP baseline within +/- 200 years of the Amaranthaceae exceedance. High Artemisia co-exceedance would indicate natural disturbance; low co-exceedance would be inconsistent with natural disturbance as the primary driver. For comparison, *Ambrosia* (ragweed, a generalist disturbance indicator that responds to any landscape opening including agricultural clearing) and Poaceae (grasses, an open-habitat indicator) were tested using the same protocol.

**Charcoal independence test.** If Amaranthaceae exceedance reflects fire-driven disturbance, charcoal influx should increase at overlapping sites. Twenty-two ENA charcoal sites from Neotoma were analysed. Sites within 50--70 km of Amaranthaceae exceedance sites were paired, and charcoal trends during the agricultural period were assessed.

**Modern surface sample validation.** One hundred modern pollen surface samples from Neotoma (ENA, 25--50 degrees N, 65--95 degrees W) were compared to the pre-3,000 BP baseline to test whether contemporary agricultural landscapes show elevated Amaranthaceae, confirming a land-use signal.

### 2.5 Archaeological cross-validation

Pre-maize Amaranthaceae exceedance sites (first exceedance at 3,000--2,000 BP) were cross-validated against the p3k14c global radiocarbon database (179,689 dates; Bird et al., 2022). For each pollen site, EAC-period radiocarbon dates (2,000--3,500 uncalibrated BP) within 50 km and 100 km were counted. A control comparison used latitude-matched points shifted 12 degrees west into the Great Plains (outside the EAC heartland).

### 2.6 Spatial null model

To test whether exceedance sites are located in areas of unusually high archaeological activity, 100 random points were generated within the EAC region (38--46 degrees N, 75--90 degrees W). For each random point, EAC-period radiocarbon dates within 50 km were counted. The distribution of date counts at random points was compared to exceedance sites using Mann-Whitney U and permutation tests (10,000 iterations).

### 2.7 European indicator comparison

To provide a positive control and contextualise the Amaranthaceae results, European-derived agricultural indicators (*Plantago lanceolata*, *Rumex*, Cerealia-type) were tested for exceedance at 111 ENA sites using the identical framework. The European analysis (331 sites, 87% exceedance; Gordon et al., 2026a [companion paper, submitted simultaneously to AAES as AAES-P-0003]) established that the exceedance framework detects pastoral agriculture when the appropriate indicators are applied.

### 2.8 Tree exceedance

Individual tree taxon exceedance was assessed for nine genera (*Quercus*, *Pinus*, *Betula*, *Acer*, *Carya*, *Picea*, *Fagus*, *Tsuga*, *Castanea*) at 112 ENA sites and 119 European sites. For each genus at each site, the baseline (>3,000 BP) mean and standard deviation were calculated. A site was classified as showing tree exceedance if any genus exceeded the baseline mean +/- 2 SD in either direction. Tree exceedance rates were compared between continents to assess whether tree composition changes are diagnostic of human management or reflect universal Holocene dynamics.

### 2.9 RPP correction and uncertainty propagation

Raw pollen percentages were converted to estimated vegetation cover using published relative pollen productivity (RPP) values: Zea mays RPP = 0.05 (Davis, 1963; Whitney et al., 2012); Ambrosia RPP = 4.09 (Matthias et al., 2015); Quercus = 1.00 (reference taxon). Monte Carlo simulation (10,000 iterations, seed = 42) propagated RPP uncertainty using uniform distributions: Ambrosia RPP ~ U(4.0, 8.0), Zea RPP ~ U(0.05, 0.20).

### 2.10 Statistical framework

Confidence intervals use the Clopper-Pearson exact method. Group differences are assessed using Mann-Whitney U (continuous) and Fisher's exact (categorical) tests. All analyses were conducted in R 4.3.x using `vegan`, `neotoma2`, and custom scripts.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Amaranthaceae exceedance: 38.7% of ENA sites --- the central finding

Of 93 ENA sites with sufficient Amaranthaceae data, 36 (38.7%) show Amaranthaceae pollen exceedance beyond the pre-3,000 BP Holocene baseline (Table 1). This signal emerges exclusively after 3,000 BP: the earliest exceedance occurs at 2,964 BP (Chatsworth Bog, Illinois), and no site shows exceedance at or before 3,000 BP. The pre-3,000 BP false-positive rate is 0% across all 93 sites, spanning more than two millennia of pre-EAC baseline.

**Table 1.** Amaranthaceae exceedance summary.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Sites analysed | 93 |
| Exceedance detected | 36 (38.7%) |
| No exceedance | 57 (61.3%) |
| False positives (before 3,000 BP) | 0 (0%) |
| Earliest exceedance | 2,964 BP (Chatsworth Bog, IL) |
| Pre-maize exceedances (3,000--2,000 BP) | 8 sites |
| Post-contact exceedances (<500 BP) | 23 sites |

Sites with deep baselines (20 or more pre-3,000 BP samples, n = 34) show an exceedance rate of 38.2% (13/34), virtually identical to the full dataset. A sensitivity analysis using a >5,000 BP baseline independently confirms 0% exceedance in the 5,000--3,000 BP window (0/89 sites; 95% CI [0%, 4.1%]). The 3,000 BP onset is robust, with no precursor signals.

### 3.3 Geographic pattern: EAC heartland concentration

The 7 pre-maize exceedance sites cluster tightly between 40.7 and 43.9 degrees N, in the Ohio/Michigan/Wisconsin/Ontario region. This geographic pattern corresponds to the archaeologically documented heartland of EAC domestication, which centres on the central Mississippi, Ohio, and Tennessee river valleys and extends into the Great Lakes region (Smith, 2006; Fritz, 2019). The latitudinal concentration at 40--44 degrees N is consistent with the distribution of archaeological sites yielding domesticated *Chenopodium berlandieri* seed assemblages.

A second phase of Amaranthaceae exceedance (<500 BP, 23 sites) is geographically broader and likely reflects post-contact weedy Amaranthaceae response to European land clearing rather than indigenous cultivation.

### 3.4 Disturbance exclusion tests

#### 3.4.1 Artemisia co-exceedance: 5.9% --- consistent with cultivation, not natural disturbance

The Artemisia co-exceedance test provides the strongest analytical discrimination between cultivation and natural disturbance. Among 34 testable Amaranthaceae exceedance sites, *Artemisia* co-exceeded within +/- 200 years at only 2 sites (5.9%; Table 3).

**Table 3.** Co-exceedance specificity results.

| Co-occurring taxon | Ecological role | Available sites | Co-exceeds | Rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Artemisia** | Drought/fire indicator (not cultivation-associated) | 34 | **2** | **5.9%** |
| Ambrosia | Generalist disturbance (includes agricultural clearing) | 35 | 30 | 85.7% |
| Poaceae | Open habitat (ambiguous) | 35 | 22 | 62.9% |

The near-absence of Artemisia co-exceedance is inconsistent with natural drought or wildfire as the primary driver of Amaranthaceae increases. If Amaranthaceae exceedance were driven by natural disturbance, Artemisia --- which increases under the same drought and fire conditions that favour wild Amaranthaceae --- should co-exceed at comparable rates. The 5.9% rate indicates that 94.4% (34/36) of Amaranthaceae exceedance sites lack the natural disturbance signature.

The high Ambrosia co-exceedance (85.7%) is expected under the cultivation hypothesis: clearing land for crops creates disturbed habitat that favours *Ambrosia*. Temporal precedence analysis shows *Ambrosia* leading Amaranthaceae by a median of approximately 43 years at co-exceedance sites --- consistent with an initial land-clearing phase followed by crop establishment.

Among the 7 pre-maize exceedance sites, 6 of 7 (85.7%) lack Artemisia co-exceedance. Only Humber Pond 3 shows a natural disturbance signature. The remaining 6 sites are classified as follows:

| Site | Classification |
|---|---|
| Chatsworth Bog | Amaranthaceae-specific (no co-exceedance) |
| Humber Pond 7 | Open-habitat (Poaceae only) |
| Central Big Creek Marsh | Open-habitat (Poaceae only) |
| Disterhaft Farm Bog | Cultivation-consistent (Ambrosia only) |
| Decoy Lake | Land clearing (Ambrosia + Poaceae) |
| Brophy Ditch | Land clearing (Ambrosia + Poaceae) |

We propose the Artemisia co-exceedance test as a new analytical tool for discriminating cultivation from natural disturbance when working with family-level pollen identifications. The logic is generalisable: for any pollen taxon that could reflect either cultivation or natural disturbance, identifying a co-occurring taxon that tracks only natural disturbance provides a specificity filter.

#### 3.4.2 Charcoal independence: no fire association

Five charcoal sites within 50--70 km of Amaranthaceae exceedance sites were analysed. None showed charcoal increases coinciding with Amaranthaceae exceedance (Table 4).

**Spatial null model.** To address the concern that the EAC heartland has archaeology everywhere, we compared the 7 exceedance sites to 100 random points within the EAC region (38--46 degrees N, 75--90 degrees W). Exceedance sites had fewer nearby EAC-period dates (mean 1.71) than random points (mean 4.93; Mann-Whitney p = 0.71; permutation p = 0.83). The Amaranthaceae signal is not an artefact of archaeological site density. The validation rests on geographic-temporal concordance with the archaeologically documented EAC heartland, not on proximity to specific archaeological sites.

### 3.6 European indicator comparison: 0% exceedance in ENA

European-derived agricultural indicators (*Plantago lanceolata*, *Rumex*, Cerealia-type) yield 0% exceedance at 111 ENA sites during the indigenous agricultural period (0/111; 95% CI [0%, 3.3%]; Table 7). The same framework applied to 331 European sites detects 87% exceedance (Gordon et al., 2026a [companion paper, submitted simultaneously to AAES as AAES-P-0003]). Tree exceedance is approximately 93% in both regions (Section 3.7) and therefore non-diagnostic.

**Table 7.** Indicator-set comparison.

| Indicator set | Taxa | ENA exceedance | Europe exceedance |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| European (Behre, 1981) | *Plantago*, *Rumex*, Cerealia | 0% (0/111) | 87% (n = 331) |
| **Indigenous (EAC)** | **Amaranthaceae** | **38.7% (36/93)** | not tested |
| Disturbance | Ambrosia | 70.3% (post-contact) | --- |

This comparison confirms that the exceedance framework is functional --- it detects European agriculture when European indicators are applied. The 0% ENA result reflects the absence of European-type pastoral taxa, not the absence of indigenous agricultural impact.

### 3.7 Tree exceedance and RPP correction (Supplementary Material S1)

Tree taxon exceedance was tested in both ENA (93.8%, 105/112 sites) and Europe (93.3%, 111/119 sites). The near-identical rates confirm that tree composition exceedance is a universal Holocene phenomenon, non-diagnostic of human management. Monte Carlo RPP correction converts the raw Ambrosia/Zea pollen ratio (40.6x) to a corrected ratio of 0.84x (95% CI: 0.33--1.68x), demonstrating that differences in raw pollen percentages between taxa largely reflect taphonomic bias. Full results are presented in Supplementary Material S1.

### 3.8 Post-contact Ambrosia: 70.3% at 200 BP --- positive control

Ambrosia exceedance was detected at 78 of 111 sites (70.3%), with a median onset of 200 BP (approximately 1750 CE). No site showed Ambrosia exceedance before 500 BP. This signal --- temporally specific to the post-contact period, geographically widespread, and with no pre-colonial precedent --- confirms that the exceedance framework is sensitive to agricultural-indicator signals in ENA pollen records when transformation occurs. It serves as a positive control: the same lake and bog records that show 0% European-indicator exceedance during the indigenous period register 70.3% exceedance once European colonial land clearing produces a signal in native disturbance taxa.

### 3.10 Chronological uncertainty and the 3,000 BP boundary

The 0% false-positive rate before 3,000 BP is a cornerstone of the temporal validation. However, radiocarbon dating uncertainties of ±100--200 years at individual sites mean that samples near the 3,000 BP boundary could shift across it. Our site selection criterion requires at least two radiocarbon dates, which is a low bar for Holocene-spanning cores. Age-depth model uncertainty could plausibly shift exceedance onset ages by several centuries. The key observation mitigating this concern is the 5,000 BP baseline sensitivity analysis: using a >5,000 BP baseline (which excludes all possible early EAC influence) yields 0% exceedance in the 5,000--3,000 BP window (0/89 sites; 95% CI [0%, 4.1%]) and identical exceedance rates post-3,000 BP. This demonstrates that the result is robust to baseline definition and not an artefact of boundary placement. Additionally, the earliest exceedance (2,964 BP at Chatsworth Bog, Illinois) postdates the 3,000 BP boundary by only 36 years, consistent with the expected tight onset. Regional-scale statistical analyses (binomial tests, permutation) are robust to random site-level chronological noise, though individual site-level onset ages should be interpreted with appropriate caution.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Evidence for pollen-based EAC detection

The results present convergent evidence consistent with pollen-based detection of EAC cultivation at a regional scale. The core signal --- 38.7% Amaranthaceae exceedance, with 0% false positives before 3,000 BP, 8 pre-maize sites at 3,000--2,000 BP clustered in the EAC heartland, and 94.4% lacking natural disturbance co-exceedance --- constitutes the strongest currently available multi-site pollen evidence consistent with EAC cultivation. Each element of the evidence is individually suggestive; their convergence strengthens the interpretation.

The temporal pattern is particularly diagnostic. Amaranthaceae exceedance appears precisely when archaeobotanical evidence places the EAC domestication complex (3,500--2,000 BP) and nowhere before. The absence of any exceedance in over two millennia of pre-EAC baseline (5,000--3,000 BP, confirmed by the alternative baseline sensitivity analysis) argues against gradual Holocene trends in Amaranthaceae abundance. Something changed at approximately 3,000 BP that pushed Amaranthaceae beyond its baseline variability at specific sites in the Ohio/Great Lakes region --- and that something is temporally and geographically coincident with the best-documented phase of EAC cultivation.

We emphasise "consistent with" rather than "demonstrates" or "proves." Family-level pollen taxonomy cannot resolve cultivated *Chenopodium berlandieri* from wild congeners, and this limitation is irreducible with existing Neotoma data (Section 4.4). The claim is that the pollen signal is consistent with the cultivation hypothesis and inconsistent with the most plausible alternative (natural disturbance), not that it constitutes definitive proof.

### 4.2 Extending McLauchlan (2003) to a regional scale

McLauchlan (2003) detected elevated *Chenopodium* pollen at a single archaeological site (Fort Ancient, Ohio) and interpreted it as consistent with local cultivation. Our analysis extends this finding in three ways. First, we move from a single archaeological site to 93 regional lake and bog cores, providing landscape-scale rather than site-specific evidence. Second, we apply a formal exceedance threshold (baseline mean + 2 SD) that quantifies departure from natural variability, replacing qualitative assessment of "elevated" pollen levels. Third, we provide multiple independent tests (Artemisia exclusion, charcoal independence, archaeological cross-validation, spatial null model) that were not available to McLauchlan.

The result is a substantial expansion: from 1 site to 36 sites with Amaranthaceae exceedance, from a single archaeological context to a regional pattern matching the EAC heartland, and from qualitative interpretation to a quantitative framework with formal false-positive assessment.

### 4.3 The Artemisia co-exceedance test: a new analytical tool

The Artemisia co-exceedance test --- demonstrating that only 5.9% of Amaranthaceae exceedance sites show concurrent natural-disturbance signals --- represents a methodological contribution applicable beyond this study. The underlying logic is straightforward: when a pollen taxon could reflect either cultivation or natural disturbance, testing for co-exceedance with a taxon that tracks only natural disturbance provides a specificity filter.

In our case, *Artemisia* serves as the natural-disturbance discriminator. It increases under drought, wildfire, and climate-driven vegetation change --- the same conditions that could promote wild Amaranthaceae --- but it is not associated with deliberate cultivation. If Amaranthaceae exceedance were driven by natural disturbance, Artemisia should co-exceed. It almost never does (5.9%).

The test is not definitive in isolation. A minority of natural disturbance events could elevate Amaranthaceae without affecting Artemisia (e.g., localised flooding). But the near-total exclusion of Artemisia at 34 of 36 sites, combined with the temporal and geographic patterns described above, creates a weight of evidence that is difficult to reconcile with purely natural drivers.

This approach is generalisable to other family-level pollen identifications in regions where cultivated and wild species co-occur. For example, elevated Poaceae pollen in tropical records (potentially reflecting rice cultivation or natural grassland expansion) could be tested against co-exceedance with known savanna indicators to discriminate cultivation from climate-driven grassland expansion.

### 4.4 Non-cultivation anthropogenic disturbance: a distinct alternative

The Artemisia exclusion test rules out natural climatic and fire-driven disturbance but does not distinguish cultivation from other forms of *anthropogenic* disturbance. Indigenous land management in ENA included deliberate burning for game management, trail clearing, and settlement construction --- activities that create disturbed ground favouring wild Amaranthaceae without involving deliberate planting (Abrams & Nowacki, 2008). The Artemisia test would yield equally low co-exceedance rates for these non-cultivation anthropogenic disturbances, because Artemisia tracks climate-driven and fire-driven disturbance, not all forms of soil disturbance.

Two observations partially address this alternative. First, the geographic concentration of pre-maize exceedance at 40--44°N in the Ohio/Great Lakes region matches the documented EAC domestication heartland (Smith, 2006), not the broader distribution of indigenous settlement across eastern North America. If settlement-associated ruderal disturbance alone were responsible, exceedance should be more geographically dispersed. Second, the temporal onset (3,000--2,000 BP) precisely matches the archaeobotanical evidence for initial EAC domestication (Fritz, 2019; Mueller, 2025), not earlier periods of hunter-gatherer occupation when non-agricultural disturbance was already widespread. Nevertheless, we cannot definitively exclude non-cultivation anthropogenic disturbance as a contributing factor. The signal is best interpreted as consistent with anthropogenic activity that includes, but may not be limited to, EAC cultivation.

### 4.5 Family-level taxonomy as the central limitation

The most important limitation of this study is the inability to distinguish cultivated *Chenopodium berlandieri* from wild congeners at the family level. Standard pollen analysis identifies Amaranthaceae (or Chenopodiaceae) grains collectively. The family includes numerous weedy species that colonise disturbed habitats without human cultivation, and pollen grain morphology at standard light-microscopy magnifications does not reliably separate cultivated from wild forms.

This limitation constrains the strength of our claims. We can state that 38.7% of sites show Amaranthaceae exceedance consistent with EAC cultivation. We cannot state that 38.7% of sites show evidence of cultivation, because the pollen grains themselves do not carry that information.

Four approaches could address this limitation in future work:

1. **Pollen morphometry.** Cultivated *C. berlandieri* produces somewhat larger grains (~20--25 um) than most wild species (~15--20 um). Re-examination of archived pollen slides from key pre-maize exceedance sites (Chatsworth Bog, Disterhaft Farm Bog) using high-resolution microscopy or image analysis could provide size-based discrimination. We note that no pollen grain size data are currently archived in Neotoma.

2. **Phytolith co-occurrence.** Co-occurring phytolith assemblages at exceedance sites could provide independent evidence for cultivation, as *Chenopodium* produces distinctive phytoliths. This would require new coring or re-analysis of existing cores.

3. **Modern analogue plots.** Establishing experimental cultivation plots of *C. berlandieri* and collecting modern pollen rain data would calibrate the expected pollen signal from known cultivated stands, providing a modern analogue for comparison with fossil assemblages.

4. **aDNA from pollen.** Emerging methods for extracting ancient DNA from pollen concentrates in sediment cores could potentially identify *Chenopodium* to species level, though this remains technically challenging for small grains.

Until these approaches are applied, the 38.7% figure remains the strongest available pollen-based evidence consistent with EAC cultivation, with family-level taxonomy as an irreducible caveat.

### 4.5 Why European indicators miss EAC: indicator selection as context

The 0% exceedance rate for European-derived indicators at 111 ENA sites, contrasting with 87% in Europe and 38.7% when indigenous indicators are applied, illustrates a broader methodological point: indicator selection determines what agriculture pollen analysis can detect. The Behre (1981) indicator list --- *Plantago lanceolata*, *Rumex*, Cerealia-type --- was developed for northwest European pastoral-arable contexts and correctly identifies the taxa associated with European cereal-pastoral economies. Its failure to detect ENA indigenous agriculture is not a flaw in the framework but an artefact of applying it outside its intended scope.

This observation has practical implications for any pollen-based investigation of non-European agricultural systems. If only European-derived indicators are tested, systems based on different crop families will be invisible regardless of their intensity. The EAC is one example; similar blind spots may exist for early African grain cultivation (Sorghum, Pennisetum), Southeast Asian tuber-based systems, and Amazonian agroforestry complexes that do not produce the pastoral-indicator assemblages that European-derived frameworks test for.

### 4.6 Implications for EAC archaeology

If the Amaranthaceae exceedance signal reflects EAC cultivation (as the disturbance exclusion tests suggest), pollen analysis offers a complementary tool to macrobotanical evidence for understanding the spatial extent of pre-maize agriculture. Macrobotanical evidence is inherently site-specific: carbonised seeds are recovered from archaeological excavations at individual sites. Pollen evidence, drawn from regional lake and bog cores, integrates signal across the landscape and can detect cultivation at sites that have not been excavated.

The 8 pre-maize exceedance sites identify locations where Amaranthaceae pollen increased beyond its Holocene baseline between 3,000 and 2,000 BP --- information that is independent of archaeological site discovery. This could guide future archaeological survey. Disterhaft Farm Bog (Wisconsin; 43.9 degrees N, 2,642 BP) and Decoy Lake (Ontario; 43.2 degrees N, 2,456 BP) lie in areas with relatively sparse macrobotanical evidence for early EAC cultivation; targeted archaeological investigation near these pollen sites could test whether the pollen signal predicts undiscovered EAC sites.

The method also provides temporal resolution that complements radiocarbon-dated macrobotanical assemblages. Pollen records from lake cores provide continuous temporal coverage, potentially detecting the onset and intensification of cultivation at finer resolution than discontinuous archaeological deposits.

### 4.7 Generalisability: applying exceedance to non-European agricultural systems

The exceedance framework developed here is applicable to any region where (a) a cultivated plant belongs to a pollen family represented in lake/bog records, (b) a pre-agricultural baseline can be defined, and (c) co-exceedance tests can discriminate cultivation from natural disturbance. We propose a general protocol:

1. **Identify the crop family** in the regional pollen record (e.g., Amaranthaceae for EAC, Poaceae for rice, Fabaceae for pulses).
2. **Define a pre-agricultural baseline** using archaeological chronology for the regional agricultural trajectory.
3. **Calculate exceedance thresholds** (baseline mean + 2 SD) for each site.
4. **Test co-exceedance** with a taxon that tracks natural disturbance but not cultivation (the "Artemisia analogue" for each region).
5. **Cross-validate** with archaeological databases (radiocarbon, macrobotanical evidence).
6. **Report false-positive rates** for the pre-agricultural baseline period.

This protocol could be applied to: (a) early African grain cultivation in savanna zones, testing Poaceae exceedance against natural grassland indicators; (b) Southeast Asian rice cultivation, testing Poaceae exceedance against known wetland-disturbance taxa; (c) Amazonian agroforestry, testing palm or other cultivated-tree family exceedance against natural forest dynamics. In each case, the European indicator framework would miss the signal entirely, while region-specific indicators might detect it.

### 4.8 Cahokia and maize: scope restriction

We restrict the scope of our Amaranthaceae exceedance claim to the pre-maize period (3,000--2,000 BP) and the sites available in the Neotoma NAPD. Two important caveats apply. First, the absence of pollen records within 200 km of major Mississippian population centres --- notably Cahokia (population approximately 20,000, with documented deforestation; Munoz et al., 2014) --- means our analysis excludes the sites where indigenous agricultural-indicator exceedance is most likely. Second, our method detects the starchy-seed EAC (Amaranthaceae-based), not maize agriculture. Zea mays pollen, with its extremely low RPP (0.05 relative to Quercus), is palynologically cryptic: only 7.2% of sites in our dataset record any Zea pollen at all. Detection of maize agriculture through pollen analysis requires specialised sieving protocols (Whitney et al., 2012) not routinely applied in Neotoma records.

### 4.9 Recommended protocol for non-European agriculture detection

Based on the combined European comparison and ENA Amaranthaceae analysis, we recommend that pollen-based agriculture detection studies:

1. Include region-specific crop indicators alongside European-derived indicators.
2. Apply exceedance thresholds (baseline mean + 2 SD) rather than presence/absence criteria.
3. Assess false-positive rates in the pre-agricultural baseline period.
4. Use co-exceedance specificity tests (the Artemisia exclusion approach) to discriminate cultivation from natural disturbance.
5. Cross-validate against archaeological databases where available.
6. Apply RPP correction for any cross-taxon comparisons.
7. Report family-level taxonomic limitations explicitly and use "consistent with" language when species-level identification is unavailable.

## 5. Conclusions

1. **Amaranthaceae exceedance at 38.7% of ENA sites provides the first multi-site pollen evidence consistent with EAC cultivation.** The signal shows 0% false positives before 3,000 BP, temporal concordance with the archaeobotanical EAC chronology, and geographic concentration in the Ohio/Great Lakes EAC heartland. Eight pre-maize sites (3,000--2,000 BP) predate maize arrival by over a millennium.

2. **The Artemisia co-exceedance test (5.9%) suggests cultivation over natural disturbance.** Only 2 of 34 Amaranthaceae exceedance sites show concurrent natural-disturbance signals. Charcoal records confirm no fire association. This disturbance-exclusion approach is a new analytical tool for family-level pollen identifications.

3. **This study extends McLauchlan's (2003) single-site precedent to a regional scale.** From one archaeological site to 93 lake/bog cores with a formal exceedance threshold, multiple disturbance-exclusion tests, and archaeological cross-validation against the p3k14c database.

4. **European-derived indicators yield 0% exceedance in ENA, confirming that indicator choice determines what pollen analysis can detect.** The same framework that detects 87% exceedance in Europe detects nothing when European pastoral-indicator taxa are applied to indigenous EAC agriculture. This demonstrates the importance of including region-specific crop indicators in any pollen-based agriculture study.

5. **A general protocol for applying exceedance analysis to non-European agricultural systems is proposed.** The combination of baseline exceedance, co-exceedance specificity testing, and archaeological cross-validation can be adapted for African, Southeast Asian, and Amazonian agricultural systems, where European indicator frameworks are similarly inappropriate.
