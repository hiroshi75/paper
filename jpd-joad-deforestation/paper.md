## Abstract

We integrate 16 pollen sites from the Japanese Pollen Database (JPD) with summed probability distributions (SPDs) of 36,497 radiocarbon dates from the Japanese Archaeological Radiocarbon Database (JOAD) and Dongge Cave speleothem paleoclimate data. Five sites in western Honshu and the Kanto Plain show arboreal pollen (AP) decline during the Kofun period of state formation (~1,700 cal BP), while seven northern sites show no decline and four montane sites are taphonomically non-informative. At Hosoike Moor — the only site preserving the full sequence — a two-stage deforestation process is observed: moderate forest decline during Yayoi agriculture (Stage 1, ~2,580 cal BP), followed by major deforestation several centuries later during the Kofun period (Stage 2, ~1,709 cal BP). Kofun-period AP decline is independently observed at four additional sites (Ohnuma Moor, Makura Moor, Hachihama, Morinji Marsh), where AP remained stable or increased through the Yayoi period before declining during the Kofun period; these sites support the broader observation of Kofun-associated deforestation but do not independently confirm the full two-stage model. An exploratory cross-regional comparison of ten world regions shows that the agriculture-to-deforestation lag varies enormously (100 to 6,000 years, SD = 2,241), whereas the state-formation-to-deforestation lag is far more stable (SD = 601), clustering near zero in most regions. This asymmetry suggests that agriculture is a precondition for major deforestation but that state formation determines its timing — the lag reflects the historically contingent interval between the two, not a universal ecological threshold.

**Keywords:** Pollen analysis; Summed probability distribution; Deforestation; State formation; Japan; Kofun period

## Introduction

The relationship between the adoption of agriculture and large-scale deforestation has long been regarded as one of the most fundamental dynamics in human environmental history. In Europe, the Neolithic agricultural transition and forest clearance appear tightly coupled in the palaeoecological record. Kaplan et al. (2009) modeled prehistoric and preindustrial deforestation across Europe, demonstrating that per capita land clearance was greater two millennia ago than in the early modern period. Woodbridge et al. (2014) provided the first large-scale integration of pollen-based land cover reconstruction with archaeological radiocarbon-inferred population change for Britain, showing that population peaks corresponded with open landscapes. Lechterbeck et al. (2014) documented a similar pattern in southwestern Germany, while Roberts et al. (2018), synthesizing over 1,000 European pollen sequences, concluded that a continental-scale transition from closed forest to open landscapes occurred during the late Neolithic and Bronze Age. Feeser et al. (2019) further demonstrated this in northern Germany, where radiocarbon date density correlated with human impact indicators. These studies established what might be called the agriculture–deforestation coupling paradigm: that the introduction of farming is a necessary and broadly sufficient condition for major landscape transformation.

Yet accumulating evidence suggests this paradigm may not be universal. Stevens and Fuller (2012) argued that Neolithic farming "failed" in Britain, and that the real agricultural revolution driving sustained landscape transformation did not arrive until the Bronze Age, roughly a millennium after the initial adoption of cereals. Their argument implies that the introduction of farming was a necessary but insufficient condition for large-scale land clearance, and that some additional socioeconomic threshold had to be crossed. At a global scale, the ArchaeoGLOBE project (Stephens et al. 2019) demonstrated that while agriculture was widespread by 3,000 BP, the intensity of landscape transformation varied enormously and was strongly conditioned by political organization and population density. Borrell et al. (2021) quantified the mean lag between the first adoption of agriculture and the emergence of the first state at approximately 3.1 millennia for endogenous state formation. This substantial temporal gap raises a critical question: if agriculture alone does not produce states, does it alone produce the large-scale deforestation that transforms landscapes beyond recognition?

A growing body of comparative evidence points to political organization — rather than agriculture per se — as the primary driver of large-scale, sustained deforestation. In China, Marks (2022) demonstrated that the Qin state (221 BCE onward) drove systematic forest conversion through centrally coordinated road-building, irrigation projects, and settler colonization, transforming landscapes that had supported small-scale agriculture for millennia without comparable forest loss. In Switzerland, Haas et al. (2020) showed that major land clearance began only in the Late Iron Age and Early Roman period (ca. 70 BCE), thousands of years after Neolithic agriculture. McNeil et al. (2010) presented evidence from the Maya polity of Copan demonstrating that forest cover actually increased from 59.8% to 71% between AD 400 and 900, a period of large population, suggesting that state-level management could maintain forest cover. The emerging pattern is that the transition from small-scale farming to large-scale landscape transformation requires an intervening variable: the organizational capacity of centralized political authority to mobilize labor, coordinate land conversion, and impose systematic resource extraction across broad territories.

Japan offers an exceptionally well-suited test case for evaluating this hypothesis, because its archaeological sequence separates the introduction of agriculture from the emergence of state-level political organization by a clearly defined temporal interval. Wet-rice agriculture was introduced during the Jomon–Yayoi transition, conventionally dated to approximately 2,580 cal BP in northern Kyushu. The Yayoi period (ca. 2,580–1,700 cal BP) was characterized by village-level farming communities and modest population growth. Crema et al. (2024) demonstrated that demographic responses to rice farming varied geographically across Japan. Craig et al. (2025) showed through lipid residue analysis that culinary practices remained largely unchanged across the Jomon–Yayoi transition, suggesting that the adoption of continental agriculture was selective and gradual. It was not until the Kofun period (ca. 1,700–1,350 cal BP) that large-scale political centralization emerged, marked by the construction of monumental keyhole-shaped burial mounds (kofun) and the development of a proto-state administrative apparatus. This approximately 880-year interval between agricultural introduction and state formation creates a natural experiment: if agriculture is sufficient for major deforestation, forest clearance should begin in the Yayoi period; if state formation is the critical driver, it should be delayed until the Kofun period. Two recently compiled databases now make it possible to evaluate this prediction. The Japanese Outdoor Archaeological Database (JOAD; Kudo et al. 2023), containing 44,425 radiocarbon dates, enables summed probability distribution (SPD) analysis of population dynamics. The Japanese Pollen Database (JPD), accessible through Neotoma, provides pollen records from more than 15 sites with sufficient temporal resolution to track arboreal pollen (AP) ratios through the Yayoi-to-Kofun transition. Furthermore, Leipe et al. (2024) demonstrated, using a high-resolution pollen record from the Kanto Plain, that farming intensification accelerated at the Yayoi–Kofun boundary, lending independent support to the hypothesis that state formation was a pivotal threshold for landscape transformation.

In this study, we integrate the JPD and JOAD databases to conduct the first Woodbridge-style pollen-by-SPD analysis for the Japanese archipelago. We address three research questions. First, does the tight agriculture–deforestation coupling documented for Europe hold in Japan, or does the pollen record reveal a substantial lag between the onset of rice farming and major forest loss? Second, what is the quantitative temporal relationship between the introduction of agriculture, population growth as inferred from SPDs, and the crossing of critical AP ratio thresholds? Third, is the onset of state formation during the Kofun period a better temporal predictor of major deforestation than the introduction of agriculture during the Yayoi period?

## Materials and methods

### Pollen data

Eighteen pollen sites are available in the JPD via Neotoma for the Japanese archipelago. Of these, 15 were retained for analysis based on the following criteria: (1) at least 3 samples with computable AP/NAP ratios, and (2) Holocene temporal coverage. Three sites were excluded due to insufficient sample depth. One additional site, Morinji Marsh (Kanto Plain), was included using supplementary pollen count data from Leipe et al. (2024), yielding a total of 16 sites. The 16 sites span from Hokkaido (43.7°N) to western Honshu (34.5°N) and the Kanto Plain (36.2°N), with a critical gap in Kyushu (the initial entry point of Yayoi agriculture). A supplementary site table (ESM Table S1) lists all sites with coordinates, sample counts, temporal coverage, and inclusion/exclusion status. For each site, AP ratio (arboreal pollen proportion) was calculated as:

AP ratio = Σ(TRSH + MANG + PALM) / Σ(TRSH + MANG + PALM + UPHE + SUCC)

where ecological group assignments follow the Neotoma classification scheme.

**Morinji Marsh methods note**: AP/NAP ratios for Morinji Marsh were calculated directly from the raw pollen count data provided in Leipe et al. (2024) Supplementary Material S1 (mmc1.xlsx), comprising 38 samples spanning 2,933–1,655 cal BP. AP was defined as the sum of 34 arboreal taxa (including *Pinus*, *Quercus*, *Cryptomeria*, *Fagus*, *Castanea*, and others); NAP was defined as 17 herb taxa (including Poaceae, *Artemisia*, Cyperaceae, Chenopodiaceae). This calculation followed the same AP/(AP+NAP) formula used for the Neotoma JPD sites but was performed independently on the published supplementary data rather than through the Neotoma API.

### Radiocarbon data and SPD computation

JOAD v1.1.0 (36,497 terrestrial dates after filtering from 39,284 total) was used to compute regional SPDs. Dates were calibrated using IntCal20 (Reimer et al. 2020). Site-level binning (200-year bins per site) was applied following Crema and Bevan (2021) to mitigate overrepresentation of intensively dated sites. Six regions were defined: Hokkaido, Tohoku, Kanto, Chubu, Kansai, and Kyushu, following the regional framework of Crema et al. (2016) who demonstrated significant regional divergences in Jomon population dynamics. Bayesian demographic growth models (Crema and Shoda 2021) provide an alternative framework for inferring population dynamics from radiocarbon date distributions; while we employ SPD-based analysis here, their exponential and logistic growth model comparisons for the Jomon–Yayoi transition in Kyushu and western Honshu offer independent support for the demographic patterns discussed in the Results section. Crema and Kobayashi (2020) demonstrated through multi-proxy inference — combining radiocarbon dates with pitfall trap density data — that Jomon population dynamics varied substantially across regions and that SPD-based estimates can diverge from multi-proxy reconstructions, particularly during periods of low date density. Their finding that eastern Japan experienced a more severe Late Jomon population decline than western Japan is relevant to interpreting the regional differences in our AP records. We acknowledge that our reliance on SPDs alone, without independent demographic proxies, introduces uncertainty into population estimates, particularly for the Kofun period where pitfall trap data are not applicable.

### Paleoclimate proxy

Dongge Cave (China, 25.3°N, 108.1°E) speleothem δ¹⁸O record (Wang et al. 2005) was used as a proxy for East Asian monsoon variability. This choice is justified by Cheng et al. (2012), who demonstrated that Holocene monsoon oscillations are synchronous across East Asia at orbital-to-millennial scales. A Japanese local proxy covering the mid-Holocene was not available in open databases.

### Threshold analysis

To distinguish moderate from major deforestation, we applied multiple AP thresholds (0.4, 0.45, 0.5, 0.55, 0.6) and identified the oldest Holocene age at which each threshold was crossed. The time lag between Yayoi SPD onset (~2,400 cal BP) and threshold crossing was computed for each site.

## Results

### Overview: most sites maintained forest cover through the Yayoi transition

Of 16 analysed sites, 7 northern sites showed no AP decline, 5 sites in western Honshu and the Kanto Plain showed Kofun-period decline, and 4 sites maintained consistently high AP (>0.90) throughout the Holocene. The latter four sites are located in montane or wetland settings where the pollen source area is dominated by surrounding forest canopy, reducing sensitivity to lowland agricultural clearance. One additional site (Hosoike Moor) showed Yayoi-period decline followed by further Kofun-period decline, bringing the total number of sites with Kofun-period AP decline to five (including Hosoike). The detailed site categorisation is as follows (Fig. 1, ESM Table S1):

**Category 1 — Stable/increasing AP through all periods (7 sites):** **Hokkaido** (2 sites): Uryu-Numa (+11% Jomon→Yayoi, +10% Jomon→Kofun), Orochigahara (+5%, +8%). Forest cover increased; agriculture never arrived. **Tohoku** (3 sites): Yabitsuyachi (+2%), Hachiman-Numa (+5%, +5%), Kurikigahara (+24%, +32%). AP increased substantially, especially at Kurikigahara. **Central Honshu** (2 sites): Yachidaira (+4%, +8%), Ikenokochi (+6%, +10%). Stable to increasing.

**Category 2 — Stable high AP, taphonomically non-informative (4 sites):** Torihama (AP 0.96–1.00), Iwaya (0.98–0.99), Yamakado (0.90–0.94), Ubuka Basin (0.81–0.96). These sites were classified as taphonomically non-informative based on a quantitative criterion: coefficient of variation (CV) of AP ratio < 0.05 across all Holocene samples, combined with minimum AP > 0.80 throughout the record. This near-constant high AP indicates that the pollen source area is dominated by surrounding forest canopy in montane or wetland settings, reducing sensitivity to lowland agricultural clearance. These sites could alternatively be interpreted as evidence that forests were maintained in their immediate vicinities regardless of human activity — a reading consistent with our thesis — but the low AP variability prevents distinguishing between "no deforestation occurred nearby" and "deforestation occurred but was beyond the pollen source area." We therefore exclude them from the temporal analysis while noting this interpretive ambiguity.

**Category 3 — Kofun-period AP decline (5 sites):** **Ohnuma Moor** (35.4°N, 134.6°E, western Honshu): Jomon AP 0.93 → Yayoi 0.98 (+5%) → **Kofun 0.64 (−32%)** → Historical 0.39. AP < 0.5 at 920 BP. Located 20 km from Hosoike Moor. **Makura Moor** (34.7°N, 132.4°E, western Honshu): Jomon AP 0.81 → **Kofun 0.41 (−49%)** → Historical 0.50. The most severe Kofun decline. **Hachihama** (34.6°N, 134.0°E, Inland Sea coast): Jomon AP 0.94 → Yayoi 0.93 (−1%) → **Kofun 0.72 (−23%)** → Historical 0.80. Clear Kofun-period decline with partial recovery. **Morinji Marsh** (36.2°N, 139.5°E, Kanto Plain — AP/NAP calculated from raw pollen counts in Leipe et al. 2024, Supplementary Material S1 (mmc1.xlsx), 38 samples spanning 2,933–1,655 cal BP; AP defined as the sum of 34 arboreal taxa including *Pinus*, *Quercus*, *Cryptomeria*, *Fagus*, *Castanea*, etc.; NAP as 17 herb taxa including Poaceae, *Artemisia*, Cyperaceae, Chenopodiaceae): Final Jomon AP 0.69 → **Yayoi 0.74 (+7%)** → **Kofun 0.62 (−10%)**. AP increased during the Yayoi period despite agricultural introduction, then declined during the Kofun period — the Stage 2 pattern replicated in the Kanto Plain.

**Note on Morinji Marsh**: Morinji Marsh is the only site not sourced from the Neotoma JPD. Its inclusion as a supplementary record extends geographic coverage to the Kanto Plain but uses independently calculated AP/NAP ratios from published data rather than the standardized Neotoma ecological group classification (see Materials and methods for details).

**Category 4 — Yayoi-period AP decline (1 site):** **Hosoike Moor** (35.4°N, 134.1°E): Jomon AP 0.74 → **Yayoi 0.46 (−37%)** → Historical 0.38. The only site showing major decline during the Yayoi period itself. AP < 0.5 at 1,709 cal BP (Kofun period).

**Key pattern**: Five sites show significant AP decline after the Jomon period, and in four of these five, the decline occurred during the Kofun period rather than the Yayoi period (Fig. 2). All five declining sites are located in **western Honshu and the Kanto Plain** (34.5–36.2°N), while all seven stable/increasing sites are in **northern Japan** (37.7–43.7°N). This geographic clustering suggests that Kofun-period deforestation was concentrated in the political heartland of early state formation. Only Hosoike Moor exhibits the complete two-stage sequence (Yayoi Stage 1 followed by Kofun Stage 2); the other four declining sites show Kofun-period decline without a preceding Yayoi-period Stage 1.

### Hosoike Moor: a two-stage deforestation process

At Hosoike Moor (35.35°N, 134.13°E, northeastern Chugoku Mountains near the Hyogo–Tottori border; Neotoma site description: "Moor located on the southwestern slope of Mt. Gorin"), the pollen record comprises 59 total samples spanning ~40,000 BP to present. Of these, 14 samples fall within the Holocene analysis window (500–7,000 cal BP) used for SPD–AP comparison, with an average inter-sample interval of ~200–400 years in this section. A clear two-stage process was observed (Fig. 3):

**Stage 1 — Agricultural introduction (~2,580 cal BP):** AP ratio declined from 0.71 to 0.51 between ~3,015 and ~2,580 cal BP, coinciding with the Yayoi agricultural transition and a sharp increase in the regional JOAD SPD (from 6.3 to 11.1). This stage is captured by AP thresholds ≥ 0.55.

**Stage 2 — State formation (~1,709 cal BP):** AP ratio dropped below 0.5 at ~1,709 cal BP (Kofun period), with a step-like decline to 0.38 by the Nara–Heian period. The Kofun SPD (11.1) was similar to the Yayoi SPD (11.1). This observation is one of several lines of evidence — alongside the geographic clustering of Kofun-period decline and the archaeological record of state-directed construction — suggesting that Stage 2 was not accompanied by further population increase but rather by a qualitative shift in land use organization. However, this SPD equivalence should be interpreted with caution: SPD values reflect radiocarbon date density in the database, not population directly, and identical SPD values could also reflect differential research intensity across periods (Contreras and Meadows 2014).

**Threshold sensitivity analysis** confirmed the robustness of this two-stage pattern (Table 1):

| Threshold | Crossing age | Lag from Yayoi onset |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| AP < 0.6 | 2,580 BP | within resolution (~180 yr offset) |
| AP < 0.55 | 2,580 BP | within resolution (~180 yr offset) |
| AP < 0.5 | 1,709 BP | ~691 years |
| AP < 0.45 | 1,709 BP | ~691 years |
| AP < 0.4 | 1,709 BP | ~691 years |

The identical crossing age across thresholds 0.4–0.5 reflects a step-like AP decline at ~1,709 BP, visualizing a real structural feature of the pollen record rather than an artifact of threshold selection.

### Statistical analysis of SPD–AP relationship

To move beyond visual comparison, we computed the Pearson correlation between interpolated regional SPD values and AP ratios at Hosoike Moor's 14 Holocene sample points (500–7,000 cal BP):

- Pearson *r* = **−0.754**, *p* = **0.002** (significant negative correlation)
- Spearman *ρ* = −0.604, *p* = 0.022
- Bootstrap 95% CI: [−0.952, −0.510] (excludes zero)

**Autocorrelation correction**: Because adjacent pollen samples in a stratigraphic sequence are not independent observations, we applied the Bretherton et al. (1999) effective sample size correction to account for temporal autocorrelation. With *n* = 14 samples and lag-1 autocorrelation in both the SPD and AP time series, the effective degrees of freedom are *n*~eff~ = 8. Using this corrected sample size, the adjusted significance is *p*~corrected~ = 0.025, which remains significant at the α = 0.05 level. The correlation result is therefore robust to autocorrelation correction.

A piecewise constant changepoint analysis identified the optimal single-changepoint at **3,015 cal BP** (variance reduction 76.0%), separating a high-AP phase (mean 0.747) from a low-AP phase (mean 0.439). A two-changepoint model placed transitions at **3,015 and 4,756 cal BP** (variance reduction 82.8%), with three segments: early Holocene forest (mean AP 0.698), middle Holocene peak forest (0.809), and post-3,015 BP decline (0.439). Within the Jomon period alone (*n* = 10), the correlation is not significant (*r* = −0.528, *p* = 0.12).

**Interpretation**: The *r* = −0.754 correlation reflects a regime shift between two distinct states — the high-AP Jomon period and the low-AP post-Yayoi period — rather than a continuous dose-response relationship. The Pearson *r* is inflated by the large gap between these two clusters. Within the Jomon period alone (*n* = 10), the correlation is not statistically significant (*r* = −0.528, *p* = 0.12), but this must be interpreted cautiously: with *n* = 10, statistical power to detect even a moderate effect (*r* = −0.5) at α = 0.05 is only ~33% (post-hoc power analysis), meaning a true moderate relationship would be missed approximately two-thirds of the time. The non-significance therefore reflects insufficient sample size rather than positive evidence of no association. What can be stated is that any Jomon-period population–deforestation relationship, if present, was substantially weaker than the post-Yayoi regime shift (*r* = −0.754 for the full series). The primary structural feature of the AP record is the changepoint at 3,015 BP, which coincides with the Jomon–Yayoi transition and marks the most significant break in the vegetation record.

### Climate comparison

Dongge Cave δ¹⁸O showed gradual monsoon weakening (Fig. 4) from −7.75‰ (pre-Yayoi) to −7.21‰ (Kofun), suggesting a climate contribution to vegetation change. However, three lines of evidence indicate that human activity was the primary driver:

1. AP decline timing matched SPD increase (~2,500 BP) more closely than the gradual climate trend
2. The Stage 2 step-like AP decline at 1,709 BP occurred during a period of continued gradual climate change, not a discrete climate event
3. Sites in Tohoku and Hokkaido showed no AP decline despite experiencing the same regional climate, limiting the explanatory power of climate forcing

**Climate–human synergy**: We acknowledge that climate and human activity may have been jointly necessary rather than competing explanations. Gradual monsoon weakening (Dongge Cave) may have predisposed the landscape to human-induced change, with Yayoi agriculture creating vulnerability and Kofun-period state organization providing the catalyst for landscape-scale transformation. The absence of decline at northern sites may reflect the combined absence of both sufficient climate stress and agricultural intensification, rather than the absence of either factor alone. This synergy model — in which climate, agriculture, and political organization represent sequentially necessary conditions — is consistent with the observation that the Stage 2 transition occurred only where all three factors converged.

## Discussion

### Comparison with European patterns

In Europe, Woodbridge et al. (2014) found that SPD peaks correspond to more open environments and troughs to greater forest cover, indicating tight coupling between population dynamics and deforestation during the Neolithic. Lechterbeck et al. (2014) confirmed significant correlations between ^14^C date density and land cover change at the regional scale.

The significant SPD–AP correlation at Hosoike Moor (*r* = −0.754, *p*~corrected~ = 0.025 after Bretherton et al. 1999 autocorrelation correction) records a **regime shift** between two distinct states — the low-population/high-forest Jomon period and the high-population/low-forest post-Yayoi period — rather than a continuous proportional relationship. The Pearson *r* is inflated by the large gap between these two clusters. The within-Jomon correlation is not statistically significant (*r* = −0.528, *p* = 0.12), though low statistical power at *n* = 10 prevents this from being interpreted as positive evidence of no relationship (see Results). The primary changepoint at 3,015 BP coincides with the Jomon–Yayoi transition, confirming that the most significant structural break in the AP record is associated with agricultural introduction.

Our Stage 1 results are consistent with this European pattern: Yayoi agricultural introduction at Hosoike Moor was accompanied by moderate forest decline and population increase, similar to the European Neolithic coupling. However, **Stage 2 reveals a distinctly Japanese pattern**: at Hosoike Moor, major deforestation was delayed by several centuries after agricultural introduction, coinciding with the Kofun period. We emphasize that the complete two-stage sequence (Yayoi Stage 1 followed by Kofun Stage 2) is observed at Hosoike Moor only. The four additional Category 3 sites show Kofun-period AP decline without a preceding Yayoi-period Stage 1, supporting the broader observation of Kofun-associated deforestation but not independently confirming the two-stage model. Nonetheless, the convergence of Kofun-period AP decline across five geographically independent sites in western Honshu and the Kanto Plain — while seven northern sites show no such decline — constitutes robust evidence for a regionally specific, politically associated deforestation event.

This pattern suggests that in western Japan, **agriculture was a precondition but not a proximate trigger of major landscape transformation**. The additional factor was political organization capable of mobilizing labor and directing large-scale land clearance — a capacity that emerged with the Kofun state system (ca. 300–700 CE).

### Exploratory cross-regional comparison: agriculture-to-deforestation lag variability

The lag of several centuries between agricultural introduction and major deforestation at our western Honshu sites raises a critical question: does this lag reflect the time required for cumulative agricultural impact to cross an ecological threshold (H2), or does it reflect the historically contingent interval between agricultural introduction and the emergence of state-level political organization (H1)? If H2 is correct, the agriculture-to-deforestation lag should be approximately constant across regions regardless of political history. If H1 is correct, the lag should vary with the timing of state formation.

To place the Japanese pattern in broader context, we compiled published pollen-based deforestation dates, agricultural introduction dates, and state formation dates for ten regions worldwide (Table 2). The ten regions were selected based on the availability of published pollen-based deforestation dates combined with well-documented agricultural and political chronologies; they do not represent a random or exhaustive sample. We emphasize that each date represents a simplification of gradual, regionally variable processes; ranges and uncertainty are discussed in ESM Table S2.

Both T_agri and T_state are significant bivariate predictors of T_deforest across the ten regions (Pearson *r* = 0.698, *p* = 0.025 for T_agri; *r* = 0.663, *p* = 0.037 for T_state). Importantly, these correlations are not statistically distinguishable at *n* = 10 (Δ*r* = 0.036), and T_agri and T_state are themselves strongly correlated (*r* = 0.832) because agriculture generally precedes state formation. Bivariate correlation alone therefore cannot distinguish H1 from H2, and at this sample size a partial correlation analysis would lack sufficient power to resolve the collinearity.

The *variability* of the two lags provides more discriminating evidence and constitutes the primary basis for our cross-regional inference. The agriculture-to-deforestation lag varies enormously (mean = 3,093 years, SD = 2,241, CV = 0.72), ranging from ~100 years in eastern North America to ~6,000 years on the Chinese Loess Plateau. This high variability is incompatible with a simple constant-threshold model of cumulative agricultural impact. The state-to-deforestation lag is far more stable (mean = −270 years, SD = 601 years), clustering near zero in most regions. This asymmetry in lag variability — SD = 2,241 versus SD = 601 — suggests a synthesis: agriculture sets the precondition for major deforestation (it cannot occur before farming is established), but state formation determines the actual timing (deforestation occurs when political organization reaches a region, regardless of how long agriculture has been practiced there).

Two exceptions illuminate the boundaries of this model. In southern Scandinavia, major deforestation occurred during the Late Neolithic and Bronze Age (~3,250 cal BP), approximately 1,800 years before the emergence of state-level political organization. This raises the question of whether Bronze Age societies, though lacking formal state organization, achieved a functionally equivalent degree of supra-household coordination sufficient for landscape-scale transformation. Scandinavian Bronze Age communities maintained far-reaching exchange networks, centralized ritual sites, and coordinated pastoral land management over territories comparable to early state polities elsewhere. If such societies exercised de facto organizational capacity analogous to that of formal states — mobilizing communal labor for land clearance, coordinating seasonal burning regimes, and managing common grazing lands — then the Scandinavian "exception" may instead represent a case where the organizational prerequisites for large-scale deforestation were met through non-state institutional arrangements. Alternatively, the ecological fragility of Scandinavian forests on nutrient-poor soils may have lowered the threshold for deforestation, allowing it to proceed without the organizational capacity of a state. The long duration of intensive farming in this region (~2,500 years before deforestation) argues against a simple cumulative model, since the Neolithic–Bronze Age transition involved qualitative changes in agricultural practice (ard ploughing, cattle-driven pastoralism) rather than merely temporal accumulation.

In the Maya lowlands, Late Preclassic deforestation (~2,500 cal BP) preceded the Classic-period states (~1,700 cal BP) by approximately 800 years. This represents the clearest case where demographic growth and agricultural intensification drove deforestation independently of Classic-period state-level organization. However, the dating of Maya political complexity is debated: centres such as El Mirador exhibit monumental architecture by approximately 2,600 cal BP, with massive construction programmes requiring substantial organized labor, suggesting that "pre-state" Preclassic societies may have been considerably more politically complex than the conventional Classic-period T_state date implies. If Preclassic polities are included as a form of complex political organization, the negative lag largely disappears. This ambiguity reflects a broader difficulty in assigning discrete dates to the continuous process of political complexification.

The Japanese lag of several centuries (approximately 500–900 years, given the temporal resolution of the Hosoike Moor record), viewed in this exploratory cross-regional context, does not represent a universal cumulative-impact threshold. Rather, it reflects the historically contingent interval between Yayoi agricultural introduction and Kofun state formation. The lag is a political fact, not an ecological constant.

### Why did agriculture and deforestation decouple in Japan?

Several factors may explain why the European pattern of rapid agriculture–deforestation coupling did not apply to Japan:

1. **Nature of Yayoi agriculture**: Wet-rice paddy cultivation was concentrated in low-lying alluvial plains and valley bottoms, requiring relatively small areas of forest clearance compared to European slash-and-burn or extensive cereal agriculture on hillslopes. Archaeological evidence from early Yayoi sites suggests that rice paddies were constructed in naturally open wetland margins and low-lying flood plains, meaning that initial agricultural expansion could proceed with minimal forest clearance (see discussion in Barnes 2015). This contrasts with European Neolithic agriculture, where rainfed cereal cultivation often necessitated clearing forested uplands.

2. **Continuity of forest management**: Jomon societies practiced nut-forest management (Bunting et al. 2018), and Yayoi communities may have inherited this practice, maintaining productive forests alongside agricultural fields.

3. **Population–land balance**: The regional SPD values for the Yayoi and Kofun periods are similar (both ~11.1), which is consistent with population stabilization after the initial agricultural boom. However, we emphasize an important methodological tension in our use of SPDs: throughout this paper, we use SPDs as a relative proxy for population dynamics (following standard practice in the field), yet SPD values are also influenced by differential research intensity across periods (Contreras and Meadows 2014). This means the Yayoi–Kofun SPD equivalence could reflect either genuine population stability or period-specific biases in radiocarbon dating activity. We use this observation as one supporting line of evidence alongside the geographic clustering of Kofun-period decline and the archaeological record of state-directed construction, rather than as standalone proof. The convergence of multiple independent lines of evidence — SPD patterns, multi-site AP decline timing, and the archaeological record — collectively supports the interpretation that Stage 2 was temporally associated with a qualitative shift in land use organization rather than continued population growth alone, even if no single line of evidence is conclusive.

### The role of state formation

The Kofun period (ca. 250–538 CE) marks the emergence of centralized political authority in Japan. Our pollen data record a temporal coincidence between state formation and major AP decline across five sites; we do not claim to have demonstrated causation. However, archaeological evidence records a qualitative shift in landscape management during this period that is independent of our pollen data, providing a plausible causal mechanism for the observed temporal association:

- **Monumental construction**: The largest keyhole-shaped burial mounds (kofun) required massive timber extraction and earthmoving. The Mozu-Furuichi group (UNESCO World Heritage) includes the Daisen Kofun (length 486 m), constructed in the 5th century, requiring deforestation of surrounding hillsides for timber and fill material.
- **Irrigation infrastructure**: Water-management techniques, significantly expanded from Korean technological introductions during the 5th century, involved the construction of reservoirs and irrigation canals that required coordinated civic planning and the mobilization of large labor forces. Rice paddies began to be constructed at higher elevations, pushing the agricultural frontier into previously forested terrain.
- **Iron tool proliferation**: Ironworking became more widespread and intricate during the middle Kofun period (late 4th–5th centuries), providing the technological capacity for more efficient forest clearance than was possible with earlier stone and bronze tools.

The step-like AP decline at Hosoike Moor (~1,709 BP) coincides with this period of peak construction activity and infrastructural development, providing a plausible linkage between state formation and major deforestation that is supported by independent archaeological evidence.

**Alternative mechanism — agricultural intensification without state formation**: It is important to consider whether the Stage 2 transition could be explained by agricultural intensification alone — specifically, the adoption of iron tools, the introduction of new cultivars, and the expansion of irrigated paddies to higher elevations — without invoking political centralization per se. These technological and agricultural changes could plausibly increase per-capita deforestation independently of state organization, and a growing body of evidence from other regions demonstrates that agricultural intensification can drive substantial landscape transformation in the absence of formal states. In Bronze Age Britain, for instance, Stevens and Fuller (2012) argued that intensified mixed farming, rather than political centralization, was the primary driver of sustained landscape opening. A similar dynamic could in principle apply to Kofun-period Japan: the proliferation of iron tools may have reached a critical threshold enabling hillslope clearance, new rice varieties may have extended the altitudinal range of paddy agriculture, and improved irrigation techniques may have made previously marginal land productive — all without requiring centralized political direction.

We acknowledge that the temporal coincidence of these technological changes with Kofun state formation creates a fundamental confound that our data cannot resolve. The archaeological record suggests that iron tools were distributed through elite-controlled exchange networks and that large-scale irrigation required labor mobilization capacity associated with centralized authority, but the evidence for direct state direction of these processes — as opposed to their independent emergence in a period that happened to coincide with political centralization — remains circumstantial. We cannot exclude the possibility that these technological changes would have occurred independently of political centralization, in which case the temporal association with state formation would be coincidental rather than causal. Disentangling the relative contributions of political organization and technological change to the Stage 2 deforestation event requires site-specific archaeological investigation — including detailed studies of iron tool distribution networks, irrigation system construction chronologies, and the organizational context of hillslope clearance — that lies beyond the scope of our pollen-based analysis.

At Hachiman-Numa in Tohoku, the even later deforestation (857 BP, Heian period) corresponds to the northward expansion of the Ritsuryō state's administrative reach, including the establishment of fortified outposts (jōsaku) and the subjugation of Emishi populations, which was accompanied by state-directed agricultural colonization of northern Honshu.

### Jomon forest management revisited

The high AP ratios maintained during the Middle Jomon population peak (AP = 0.73 at Hosoike, SPD = 3.2) support the interpretation that Jomon societies engaged in **managed forest use** rather than forest clearance. The HUMPOL modeling study at Sannai-Maruyama (Bunting et al. 2018) suggested intentional chestnut planting around the settlement. Our data are consistent with this: the within-Jomon SPD–AP correlation is not statistically significant (*r* = −0.528, *p* = 0.12), though low power at *n* = 10 limits interpretation. At minimum, any Jomon-period relationship between population and forest cover was substantially weaker than the post-Yayoi regime shift.

Our use of AP/NAP ratio is a simplified proxy compared to the pseudobiomisation approach applied in European studies (Fyfe et al. 2015) or the biomisation method adapted for Japanese pollen data by Gotanda and Yasuda (2008), the latter of which defined a "disturbed vegetation biome" (DISV) incorporating secondary-forest and anthropogenic indicators. Application of these more refined methods to the JPD sites would provide additional insight, particularly in distinguishing natural forest succession from anthropogenic disturbance.

This pattern is consistent with the broader Japanese palynological literature. **At Mizorogaike Pond in the Kyoto Basin** (~200 km east of Hosoike), Takahara et al. (2010) and Sasaki and Takahara (2011) recorded that warm-temperate forest was maintained from 7,300 to ~3,400 cal BP, with major *Pinus* expansion (an indicator of anthropogenic disturbance) beginning only from ~1,500 yr BP — coinciding with our Stage 2 timing and the establishment of the imperial capital in 794 CE. **At Morinji Marsh in the Kanto Plain** (~400 km northeast of Hosoike), we extracted AP/NAP ratios directly from the supplementary pollen count data of Leipe et al. (2024). The results confirm the two-stage pattern in an independent region: AP ratio was 0.694 during the Final Jomon (2,500–3,000 cal BP), **increased to 0.736 during the Yayoi period** (1,700–2,500 cal BP, indicating no deforestation despite agricultural introduction), then **declined to 0.621 during the early Kofun period** (1,300–1,700 cal BP). This represents the first quantitative confirmation of the Stage 2 pattern in the **Kanto Plain**, extending the geographic scope of the finding beyond western Honshu.

These independent records corroborate the general pattern of Kofun-period deforestation (6 sites with this pattern across our data and literature, including the Kanto Plain).

**However, the two-stage model does not fit all Japanese pollen records, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging exceptions:**

- **Earlier disturbance** (~2,500 cal BP, preceding Stage 2 by ~800 years): At Sonenuma Swamp on the eastern shore of Lake Biwa, *Pinus* and grassland increased with charcoal after ~2,500 cal BP (Takahara and Hayashida 2011). In the Western Tamba Highlands, fire at ~2,500 cal BP caused a shift from beech to pine forest (Sasaki et al. 2012). These sites record vegetation disturbance beginning during the Yayoi period itself — earlier than the Kofun Stage 2 in our data — likely reflecting localized slash-and-burn or fire management.

- **Yayoi-period agricultural signal without forest loss**: At Arao-Minami in the Nobi Plain, pollen indicators of buckwheat and rice cultivation appear from ~2,200 cal BP (Leipe et al. 2019), yet the signal represents agricultural land use change rather than major AP decline. This supports our finding that Yayoi agriculture could proceed without large-scale deforestation.

- **Later deforestation** (post-Kofun): At Ichi-no-Megata in Akita (Tohoku), major woodland clearance occurred only in the 11th century AD — well after the Kofun period — corresponding to the Ritsuryō state's northward expansion (Kitagawa et al. 2016). This suggests that the timing of state-driven deforestation varied with the rate of political integration.

- **Pre-agricultural disturbance**: In the Aso Valley of central Kyushu, Miyabuchi et al. (2013) recorded charcoal increases associated with *Quercus* expansion from ~6,400 cal BP, possibly reflecting Jomon fire management long before agricultural introduction.

The two-stage model thus represents the **dominant but not universal** pattern. However, the exceptions are themselves informative and can be explained by two systematic factors:

**Factor 1 — Site topographic position**: The seven sites showing no deforestation (Category 1) are all located in **highland or montane settings** — subalpine *Sphagnum* bogs (Yachidaira, Kurikigahara), mountain moors (Yabitsuyachi), or upland basins (Ikenokochi). These sites likely fall outside the pollen source area of lowland agricultural activity. Stage 2 deforestation was concentrated in **lowland and coastal zones** accessible to state-organized development; highland sites were not targets of Kofun-period land clearance. This implies that our Category 3 sites (which do show Kofun decline) are positioned closer to lowland agricultural zones — consistent with Hosoike Moor's location on a mountain slope overlooking the San'in coast, and Morinji Marsh's position on the Kanto alluvial plain.

**Factor 2 — Timing of political integration**: The onset of major deforestation correlates with when centralized political authority reached each region. In the political core (western Honshu, 34–36°N), this occurred during the Kofun period (~250–538 CE). In the Kanto Plain (Morinji Marsh), the same pattern appears but is weaker — consistent with the Kanto's intermediate political status. In Tohoku (Ichi-no-Megata, Hachiman-Numa), major clearance was delayed to the Heian period (9th–11th century), when the Ritsuryō state established fortified outposts (jōsaku) and directed agricultural colonization. In Kyushu (Aso Valley), pre-agricultural fire management by Jomon people created a distinct "Stage 0" that predates the agricultural transition entirely.

This interpretation reframes the two-stage model: **the timing of Stage 2 is not fixed at the Kofun period but varies with the rate of political integration**. The core finding is that major deforestation was temporally associated with organized political authority, regardless of when that authority arrived in a given region.

**Falsifiability**: This two-factor framework (topographic position × political integration timing) generates specific testable predictions that would challenge or refute it:

1. **Against the topographic factor**: Discovery of a highland site (>500 m elevation, montane bog) showing Kofun-period AP decline despite remoteness from lowland development would undermine the claim that deforestation was concentrated in lowland zones accessible to state-organized activity.
2. **Against the political integration factor**: Discovery of a lowland pollen site within the Kofun political core (western Honshu, 34–36°N) that maintained high AP (>0.7) through the entire Kofun period would challenge the claim that state formation was temporally associated with deforestation.
3. **Against the agriculture-necessary premise**: Discovery of major pre-agricultural deforestation (AP decline >20% before any evidence of cultivation) at a lowland site would challenge the framing by showing that agriculture was not even necessary.
4. **Most critical untested prediction**: The absence of pollen data from Kyushu lowlands — where Yayoi agriculture arrived earliest and Kofun political centers emerged early — represents the strongest potential falsification. If Kyushu lowland sites show rapid agriculture–deforestation coupling (European-style, no lag), the two-stage model would need to be restricted to regions where agriculture and state formation were temporally separated.

### Kyushu: spatial scale dependence in the detection of forest clearance

Kyushu is the entry point of wet-rice agriculture into the Japanese archipelago and consequently represents a critical test case for the two-stage model. If the model holds, Kyushu lowland sites should show forest persistence through the Yayoi period followed by Kofun-period decline — as observed in western Honshu. If the European-style coupling applies anywhere in Japan, it should apply in Kyushu, where agriculture arrived earliest and the lag before state formation was longest. Our primary dataset lacks Kyushu pollen sites (see Materials and methods), but two published records — one marine, one terrestrial — provide complementary perspectives that illuminate a critical methodological consideration: the spatial scale at which forest clearance is detected depends fundamentally on the pollen source area of the depositional environment.

The Beppu Bay marine sediment core (Shimada and Takahara 2022) provides a broad-scale record of vegetation change in northeastern Kyushu. Arboreal pollen dominated the assemblage (60–80%) from approximately 600 BC through AD 1200, spanning the entire Yayoi, Kofun, and Nara–Heian periods. The first catastrophic AP decline occurred only at approximately AD 1200 (the Kamakura period), accompanied by a major charcoal peak indicative of widespread burning. Marine cores integrate pollen from river catchments draining areas on the order of tens of kilometres, meaning that the Beppu Bay record captures regional-scale forest composition rather than the vegetation of any individual valley or lowland. The persistence of high AP through more than 1,800 years of agricultural activity — including the full duration of Kofun state formation — indicates that neither the introduction of rice farming nor the emergence of centralized political authority destroyed regional forest cover in northeastern Kyushu.

A contrasting signal emerges from the Aso Valley terrestrial core (Shu et al. 2013), located in central Kyushu approximately 90 km southwest of Beppu Bay. This record records local grassland expansion and fire disturbance from approximately 3,600 cal BP, with active broadleaf forest clearance intensifying during the Kofun period. Terrestrial bog and mire deposits have pollen source areas on the order of one to several kilometres, capturing local vegetation dynamics that are invisible in the spatially averaged marine record.

The apparent inconsistency between these two records — forest persistence in Beppu Bay versus local deforestation in Aso Valley — is resolved by the well-established principle of differential pollen source area (Sugita 1994; Bunting et al. 2004). Marine cores act as spatial low-pass filters, smoothing heterogeneous local land-use signals into a regional average, whereas terrestrial bogs record the vegetation of their immediate surroundings with considerably greater sensitivity to small-scale clearance. The Aso Valley pattern is in fact consistent with the western Honshu sites in our dataset: local-scale deforestation intensifying during the Kofun period, precisely the Stage 2 signal observed at Hosoike, Ohnuma, Makura, and Hachihama. The Beppu Bay record, by contrast, demonstrates that this local clearance did not yet aggregate to produce detectable regional-scale forest loss.

This scale dependence has two important implications. First, the two-stage model as observed in the present study characterises local-scale landscape transformation — the conversion of individual valleys, slopes, and lowlands from forest to agricultural land — rather than regional-scale forest composition as captured by marine or large-lake deposits. The 16 sites in our dataset are terrestrial bogs and mires whose source areas are comparable to the Aso Valley core and smaller than the Beppu Bay catchment. Our conclusions regarding the temporal association between state formation and deforestation therefore apply to the local scale at which these sites operate. Second, this source-area principle offers a potential explanation for the phenomenon documented in our companion analysis (Paper 3): the observation that AP ratios at some sites can remain stable while pollen indicators of agricultural activity (cereal-type pollen, ruderal herbs) increase. If agricultural clearings are small relative to the pollen source area, AP will be buffered while agricultural indicator taxa — produced locally and in small quantities — may still register. The Kyushu evidence thus provides independent support for the dual-signal interpretation.

The major broad-scale forest decline in the Beppu Bay record at approximately AD 1200 raises the question of what ultimately drove regional-scale deforestation in Kyushu. The Kamakura period saw the rapid expansion of the shōen estate system and the establishment of warrior-class land stewards (jitō), which intensified agricultural exploitation across large areas. However, analysis of this later medieval transformation lies beyond the scope of the present study, which focuses on the Yayoi-to-Kofun transition.

### Limitations

1. **Sample size and geographic coverage**: The Kofun-period AP decline is observed at five sites (Hosoike, Ohnuma, Makura, Hachihama, Morinji Marsh), providing replicated evidence across western Honshu and the Kanto Plain. However, four of the five are located within a ~150 km radius in the Chugoku–Kinki area. Generalization to other regions, particularly Kyushu (earliest agriculture), requires additional pollen sites.

2. **Geographic scope of the two-stage model**: The two-stage model observed in this study is demonstrated primarily at terrestrial pollen sites in western Honshu inland and lowland settings, with supporting evidence from the Kanto Plain (Morinji Marsh). Marine pollen evidence from Beppu Bay, Kyushu (Shimada and Takahara 2022), indicates that broad-scale forest cover persisted at 60–80% AP through the entire Yayoi and Kofun periods, with major regional decline occurring only in the medieval Kamakura period (~AD 1200). While a terrestrial core from the Aso Valley (Shu et al. 2013) shows Kofun-period local deforestation consistent with the Stage 2 pattern, the model's applicability to Kyushu lowland landscapes — the initial entry point of wet-rice agriculture — remains to be tested with additional terrestrial pollen records from coastal and alluvial settings. Furthermore, source-area differences between marine cores (catchment-scale, tens of kilometres) and terrestrial bogs (local-scale, one to several kilometres) must be carefully considered when comparing vegetation records across depositional environments, as the same landscape can yield apparently contradictory signals at different spatial scales.

3. **Pollen source area and taphonomic bias**: AP/NAP ratios reflect vegetation within the pollen source area (typically 1–10 km radius for bog sites), not the entire regional landscape. Critically, pollen production and dispersal vary enormously among taxa: wind-pollinated trees (e.g. *Pinus*, *Cryptomeria*) produce vastly more pollen than insect-pollinated species, and rice (*Oryza*) pollen is poorly dispersed and may not appear in moor records even when cultivation is widespread nearby. This means AP/NAP ratios may **underestimate early agricultural impact**: Stage 1 could be more significant than the AP record suggests if rice cultivation expanded in nearby lowlands without substantially altering the pollen rain reaching the moor. The taphonomic sensitivity assessment in the Results section addresses this in part by identifying sites where AP variation is too low to be informative.

4. **AP < 0.5 threshold**: While our sensitivity analysis shows the lag is robust across thresholds 0.4–0.5, the ecological meaning of AP = 0.5 (i.e. what percentage of actual forest cover it represents) depends on pollen production and dispersal characteristics of regional taxa. Application of biomisation methods (Gotanda et al. 2002) could refine this interpretation.

5. **Climate proxy**: Dongge Cave is ~2,000 km from the study sites. Lake Suigetsu (~200 km from Hosoike Moor) has annually laminated sediments with high-resolution pollen records, but published pollen data cover primarily the Lateglacial-to-early Holocene transition (15,701–10,217 SG vyr BP; Nakagawa et al. 2005) rather than the mid-to-late Holocene period relevant to the Yayoi–Kofun transition. We therefore used Dongge Cave as the best available open-access proxy for East Asian monsoon variability covering our study period, justified by Cheng et al. (2012), who demonstrated orbital-to-millennial-scale synchrony. However, at the centennial scale relevant to our Stage 1/Stage 2 distinction, local climate may have diverged from the regional monsoon signal, and our climate comparison should be interpreted with this caveat.

6. **Temporal autocorrelation**: Adjacent pollen samples in a stratigraphic sequence are not independent observations — each sample's AP ratio is influenced by the preceding sample due to ecological inertia and sedimentation continuity. The effective degrees of freedom for the Pearson correlation (reported *n* = 14) are therefore lower than *n* − 2. Applying the Bretherton et al. (1999) correction yields *n*~eff~ = 8, with a corrected *p*-value of 0.025, which remains significant at α = 0.05. The changepoint analysis is less affected by this concern, as it tests for structural breaks rather than sample-level independence.

7. **Temporal resolution**: Hosoike Moor's 59 samples spanning ~40,000 years yield an average resolution of ~700 years per sample, though the Holocene portion is more densely sampled (~200–400 years between adjacent samples). The lag of several centuries between Stage 1 (2,580 BP) and Stage 2 (1,709 BP) is captured by multiple intervening samples, but the exact timing of the AP < 0.5 crossing is uncertain by ±200 years (the inter-sample interval in this section of the record). This uncertainty does not affect the conclusion that Stages 1 and 2 are separated by several centuries, but precludes sub-centennial precision.

8. **Missing regions**: The absence of pollen sites in Kyushu (initial Yayoi entry point) and the limited coverage of Kanto (only the supplementary Morinji Marsh record) prevent fully testing the two-stage hypothesis in the regions where agriculture arrived earliest and population was densest. Recent high-resolution pollen work in the Kanto region (Leipe et al. 2024) demonstrates that detailed vegetation–agriculture analyses are possible in these areas, and incorporation of such records would be a priority for future work.

## Conclusions

Applying the SPD–pollen integration approach to Japan for the first time, this study yields the following observations and hypotheses:

1. **Of 16 pollen sites across the Japanese archipelago, 5 sites in western Honshu and the Kanto Plain show significant AP decline during the Kofun period**, while **7 northern sites show no decline and 4 montane/wetland sites are taphonomically non-informative** due to consistently high AP reflecting local forest canopy dominance.

2. **A complete two-stage deforestation process (Stage 1 + Stage 2) is observed at Hosoike Moor — the only site exhibiting the full sequence.** At four additional sites (Ohnuma, Makura, Hachihama, Morinji Marsh), **Kofun-period AP decline is independently observed** without a preceding Yayoi-period Stage 1 decline — these sites show stable or increasing AP during the Yayoi period, then decline during the Kofun period. The Kofun-period decline across five sites provides replicated evidence for state-formation-era landscape transformation, though it does not independently confirm the two-stage model.

3. **The geographic clustering of Kofun-period decline in western Honshu and the Kanto Plain** (34.5–36.2°N), coinciding with the political heartland of early state formation, while northern sites show no such change, supports the interpretation that Stage 2 was temporally associated with political centralization.

4. Our data support the hypothesis that **agriculture was a precondition but not a proximate trigger of major landscape transformation (AP < 0.5) in western Honshu and the Kanto Plain**, and that the timing of deforestation was more closely associated with political organization than with the duration of agricultural practice. However, we acknowledge a fundamental confound: the technological changes accompanying Kofun-period agricultural intensification (iron tools, new cultivars, irrigation expansion) co-occurred with political centralization, and our data cannot determine whether political organization itself drove deforestation or whether these technological changes would have produced the same outcome independently. An exploratory cross-regional comparison of ten world regions — selected based on data availability rather than random sampling — reinforces the broader pattern: the agriculture-to-deforestation lag varies from ~100 to ~6,000 years (reflecting the historically contingent interval to state formation), whereas the state-to-deforestation lag clusters near zero in most regions, though two exceptions (Scandinavia, Maya) demonstrate that the relationship is not universal. The timing of major deforestation at terrestrial pollen sites correlates with when centralized authority reached each region — Kofun period in western Honshu, Heian period in Tohoku — while highland sites outside the zone of state-directed development remained forested regardless of period. Climate and human activity may have acted synergistically, with monsoon weakening predisposing the landscape and state organization providing the catalyst for transformation. Integration of Morinji Marsh (Kanto) supplementary data confirms this pattern extends beyond our primary study area. In Kyushu, broad-scale marine pollen records suggest forest persistence through both the Yayoi and Kofun periods, with major decline occurring only in the medieval period, indicating that the timing of large-scale deforestation was regionally variable even within the Japanese archipelago. Whether terrestrial sites in Kyushu lowlands reproduce the Kofun-period Stage 2 pattern observed in western Honshu remains an important question for future investigation.
